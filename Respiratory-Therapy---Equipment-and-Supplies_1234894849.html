VARLab : Respiratory Therapy - Equipment and Supplies

Created by Stacey Dineen (Unlicensed), last modified on Feb 20, 2025

In the first module of the Respiratory Therapy DLX, the primary goal for the student is to build confidence and gain familiarity with the equipment and supplies required for bronchoscopy. In this module, the learner will practice recognizing and identifying the following required equipment and supplies.

The learner will have the opportunity to explore the Bronchoscopy Tower and Supplies by clicking on items to see the Item Name and Item Description for each item. This interaction is very similar to Ambulance Orientation - Explore Mode. Items of interest will be highlighted on hover so that the learner knows which items they can learn about. When clicked, an information panel will be displayed with the Item Name and Item Description as the most prominent features. All of the text for the UI must be copied from the Respiratory Therapy Script document in SharePoint.

Interactable, but does not move. When pressed, the ring around the button lights up green to indicate Power ON, but the button does not really move. Medications - All Interactable

Unlike some of the other supplies or equipment in this DLX, ALL of the medications listed here are required to be interacted with.

Most of the liquid medications have a lid that twists on and off - the lid must be able to be removed, and a 10cc syringe must be able to be inserted into the opening (neck) of the bottle to draw up the liquid inside.

Some liquid medications may be drawn up by inserting a blunt tip dispensing needle (attached to the syringe tip) through the rubber port on the non-removable lid.

Only the epinephrine is in a glass ampule. The ampule does not have a lid - you literally break it in half to get it open, it is designed to snap off at the narrow part of the neck. The liquid inside is drawn up using a syringe with a blunt needle attached (the neck of the ampule is definitely not large enough to insert a syringe, so just the needle goes in through the broken-off spot), and then the entire ampule and broken off neck are discarded in a sharps container.

Note that medications are often different colours, and the colour of the liquid is the primary way of identifying which syringe contains which medication, once the meds are drawn up (they are no longer in their original container so the label won’t help at that point).

Some liquid medications can be sprayed using an atomizer attachment. In that case, no syringe is needed, but the cap for the medication would be removed and the atomizer would be clicked into place to make the medication ready for use before the start of a procedure.

Medication labels must include the legible NAME and DOSAGE/STRENGTH as these are the key identifiers that an RT would use to select the correct medication as ordered by the physician. Brand name and logo, quantity of medication in the bottle, DIN, etc. are not required info when recognizing and identifying medications in this DLX. For example: Lidocaine Topical Solution 4% or 40mg/mL is the critical information that must be legible on the label, but the trade name “Lidodan” and company name ODAN & logo are not useful.
