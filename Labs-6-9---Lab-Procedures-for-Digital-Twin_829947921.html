VARLab : Labs 6-9 - Lab Procedures for Digital Twin

Created by Stacey Dineen (Unlicensed), last modified on Apr 02, 2024

This page captures the lab procedures and expected outcomes for Labs 6-9 using the Digital Twin trainer board. Unlike the Procedural Labs 1-5, these labs will be completely unguided. Students will need to refer to external course materials such as a Lab workbook or similar document to read the procedure instructions, there will be NO user prompts in-game. Likewise, the digital twin labs will not have any Knowledge Checks or feedback in-game. Students will use the virtual trainer board in a very similar way to how they use the physical trainer board, without the risk and difficulty of carrying the trainer board home.

  • Lab 6: Measuring Current Flow in a Series Circuit
  • Lab 7: Measuring Current Flow in a Parallel Circuit
  • Lab 8: Measuring Voltage Drop in a Series Circuit
  • Lab 9: Measuring Voltage Drop in a Parallel Circuit

Lab 6: Measuring Current Flow in a Series Circuit

The main objective in this lab is for the student to practice measuring current flow in a series circuit. They will start by building a series circuit that is very similar to Lab 3 and then practice measuring current flow in the circuit. Through this practice, the student will gain understanding about how multiple light bulbs connected in series will share the available current between them. When a second bulb is added in series, the current flow at the fuse will decrease.

Remember that there are no in-game user prompts, voiceover instructions, feedback, circuit diagrams, or conclusion for the digital twin labs. Students will be working from a separate document to read instructions and record results (if needed).

Lab 7: Measuring Current Flow in a Parallel Circuit

The main objective in this lab is for the student to practice measuring current flow in a parallel circuit. They will start by building a simple series circuit that is very similar to Lab 2, before adding another bulb in parallel and measuring current flow at the fuse. The student will observe that two bulbs connected in parallel act as two separate loads, doubling the current flow at the fuse.

Remember that there are no in-game user prompts, voiceover instructions, feedback, circuit diagrams, or conclusion for the digital twin labs. Students will be working from a separate document to read instructions and record results (if needed).

Lab 8: Measuring Voltage Drop in a Series Circuit

The main objective in this lab is for the student to practice measuring voltage drop in a series circuit to identify and resolve a problem. Instead of building a circuit, the student will begin this lab with a ready-made series circuit on the board (the same series circuit that was used at the end of Lab 6, but the bulbs are very dimly lit). The learning objective is to practice measuring voltage drops at various points around the circuit in order to identify a fault in the circuit. The student should be able to isolate the fault in the black lead connecting the second lightbulb to the Battery - terminal. This fault will be visible on the board because the lightbulbs will be very dimly lit, and the lab will lead the student through the process of finding the fault. Note that the black lead itself will appear completely normal.

A major element of making Labs 8 & 9 engaging for students will be the “mystery solving” aspect of searching for the fault.

Using the Digital Twin for this lab means that we will not have any user prompts or in-game feedback to lead or encourange the learner. They will be allowed to freely explore the circuit and test components and connections in any order that they wish, guided by their lab workbook. There is no right or wrong way for them to test these circuits, but they will be called upon to apply what they have learned about the trainer board components and series/parallel circuits to solve the mystery and repair the circuits!

N/A

Trainer board is shown with the same series circuit that was on the board at the end of Lab 6, except for 2 important differences:

  1. The voltage regulator is set to 14V

  2. The light bulbs at L1 and L2 are just barely lit - they are so dim that something is obviously wrong.

The lab workbook will instruct the student to observe that the voltage regulator is set to 14V, and the light bulbs are very dimly lit.

  • Student will observe a familiar series circuit and notice that it is not functioning as expected.

  • NOTE: All of the components on the board should look completely fine - not damaged or blackened or anything. Most connection issues are not visible on the outside of the component, lead, or jack, so the only observable problem in this circuit is that the light bulbs are very dimly lit.

All of these test steps are optional, and may be performed in any order. They are described here so that we have an understanding of how each voltage drop test would be set up, and what the expected DMM reading would be.

The DMM is expected to be set to Volts DC for all of these tests, but there are no user prompts in-game to confirm this.

Test the source voltage

  • To confirm source voltage

    • Connect Battery + to Volt-Ohm port on DMM

    • Connect Battery - to COM port on the DMM

  • DMM reading is approximately 14V

  • Student is able to rule out source power deficit as the cause of the dim bulbs

Test the voltage drop across a positive (red) lead

N/A

  • To test voltage drop across any positive (red) lead, in order to to check if there is any unexpected resistance on a lead.

    • Stack test lead from the jack at one end of a positive lead to the COM port on the DMM

    • Stack test lead from the jack at the other end of a positive lead to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be 0.1V or less.

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the positive leads in this circuit are not faulty, and the very tiny voltage drops shown on the DMM are normal and expected.

  • NOTE: This procedure is the same for any of the positive leads in Lab 8. The lab workbook will direct the student to find the fault in the circuit, but it may not be necessary for them to test every single component. They may test some or all of the leads on the board, as many times as they wish.

Test the voltage drop across the fuse

  • To test voltage drop across the fuse, in order to check if there is any unexpected resistance at the fuse.

    • Stack a test lead at the right side of the fuse to the COM port on the DMM.

    • Stack a test lead at the left side of the fuse to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be close to 0.0V

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the DMM reading is normal and expected, and that the fuse is not faulty.

Test the voltage drop across the switch

  • To test voltage drop across the switch, in order to check if there is any unexpected resistance at the switch.

    • Stack a test lead at the right side of the switch to the COM port on the DMM.

    • Stack a test lead at the left side of the switch to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be close to 0.0V

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the DMM reading is normal and expected, and that the switch is not faulty.

Test the voltage drop across a light bulb

  • To test voltage drop across a light bulb, in order to check if there is any unexpected resistance at the light bulb.

    • Stack a test lead at L1-B (or L2-D) to the COM port on the DMM.

    • Stack a test lead at L1-A (or L2-C) to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be approximately 4.0V

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the DMM reading is normal and expected, and that the light bulbs in this circuit are not faulty.

NOTE: This DMM reading is lower than expected, but it reflects the very dimly lit condition of the bulbs.

With no fault in the circuit, two light bulbs sharing 14V would each be fairly brightly lit, with slightly less than 7V voltage drop for each bulb. Refer to this image to compare the appearance of these 4A bulbs at 4V vs 7V.

Test the voltage drop across the FAULTY negative (black) lead

  • To test voltage drop across the negative (black) lead, in order to to check if there is any unexpected resistance on that lead.

    • Stack test lead from the jack at one end of the negative lead to the COM port on the DMM

    • Stack test lead from the jack at the other end of the negative lead to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • Note that this procedure is the same as testing the positive leads.

  • The reading on the DMM should be approx 6V.

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the negative lead in this circuit is faulty, because the voltage drop of greater than 0.1V is unexpected.

NOTE: It is normal for a lead to have very low resistance, resulting in a voltage drop of 0.1V or less. This faulty lead shows a voltage drop of approx 6V which is clearly causing the problem in this circuit.

With a total of 14V available, the high resistance of the black lead is using 6V, leaving only 8V to power the two light bulbs at L1 and L2 in series, so that they only get approx 4V each.

This is the end of the expected voltage drop test descriptions for Lab 8. The student may perform these tests as many times as they wish, in any order, and they may choose to repeat the tests after repairing the fault in this series circuit. The student may choose to test components not listed here, but those readings (if any) are irrelevant to this lab and do not require description here.

It is expected that all test leads should be removed after testing is completed, but this is not enforced.

Repairing the fault

N/A

The lab workbook will instruct the student to replace the faulty component, once they have identified it. In this circuit, the student may replace any of the components (light bulbs, fuse, or any leads) but nothing will change unless they replace the negative (black) lead connecting L2-D to Battery -.

Here is the expected procedure to replace the faulty negative (black) lead:

  • Optionally, the student may open the switch at SW1 (this is best practice but is not enforced)

  • Student will select and remove (click trash can icon) the black lead connecting L2-D to the Battery - terminal.

  • Student must select a new black lead from the bundle, and connect it from L2-D to the Battery - terminal.

  • If the switch is open, the student must close it

  • When the circuit is completed with the new negative (black) lead in place, both light bulbs will immediately be lit brightly! This will be a very noticeable change, because the bulbs were just barely lit at all when the faulty negative lead was in place.

  • Student is able to replace the faulty lead on the board without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the series circuit is now functioning exactly as expected, with the two light bulbs equally sharing approx 14V of available source power, and brightly lit.

Lab 9: Measuring Voltage Drop in a Parallel Circuit

The main objective in this lab is for the student to practice measuring voltage drops in a parallel circuit to identify and resolve faults in a circuit. Instead of building a circuit, the student will begin this lab with a ready-made parallel circuit on the board (very similar parallel circuit to the one that was used at the end of Lab 7). The learning objective is to practice and gain confidence with measuring voltage drops at various points around a parallel circuit in order to identify faults in the circuit.

Right from the start, the student will easily notice that there is an issue because the light bulbs will not be equally and bright lit, as expected. Students will practice testing the entire circuit, even after they have found a problem, because there can be multiple unrelated problems on the same circuit. Because this is a Digital Twin lab, there will be no user prompts or feedback in-game, and students will need to follow their lab workbook to complete the lab.

Note that it does not matter in which order the faulty components are found or replaced. The circuit will not function as expected until both faults are repaired, so the order is not important.

Test the voltage drop across the FAULTY fuse at F1

  • To test voltage drop across the faulty fuse, in order to check if there is any unexpected resistance at the fuse.

    • Stack a test lead at the right side of the fuse to the COM port on the DMM.

    • Stack a test lead at the left side of the fuse to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be close to 6.5V

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the DMM reading is NOT normal and this fuse is faulty.

NOTE: A blown fuse is not the same as a faulty fuse. In this case, the faulty fuse has abnormally high resistance of 6.5V. This could be caused by a degraded or corroded contact, or a manufacturing defect in the fuse. This is not a blown fuse - it should appear to be completely normal.

    • Stack a test lead at the left side of the switch to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be close to 0.0V

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the DMM reading is normal and expected, and that the switch is not faulty.

Test the voltage drop across the FAULTY light bulb at L1 or L2 (completely dark bulb)

  • To test voltage drop across the FAULTY light bulb, in order to check if there is any unexpected resistance at the light bulb.

    • Stack a test lead at L1-B (or L2-D) to the COM port on the DMM.

    • Stack a test lead at L1-A (or L2-C) to the Volt-Ohm port on the DMM.

  • The reading on the DMM should be approximately:

    • 14V if the fuse at F1 has been replaced

    • 7.5V if the fuse at F1 is faulty

  • Student is able to test the voltage drop across a conductor without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the DMM reading is normal and expected, but that the light bulb must be faulty since it is dark.

  • Faulty light bulb (open filament) is installed at L1 or L2

NOTE: The voltage drop reading on the DMM is exactly the same whether the bulb is faulty or good.

Repairing the fault

N/A

The lab workbook will instruct the student to replace the faulty component, once they have identified it. In this circuit, the student may replace any of the components (light bulbs, fuse, or any leads) but nothing will change unless they replace the faulty components:

  • Faulty light bulb at L1 or L2

  • Faulty fuse at F1

Here is the expected procedure to replace the faulty light bulb:

  • Optionally, the student may open the switch at SW1 (this is best practice but is not enforced)

  • Student must click on the faulty light bulb. It will be removed automatically when they click.

  • Student must click on the empty socket to place a new 4A light bulb in that position.

Here is the expected procedure to replace the faulty fuse:

  • Optionally, the student may open the switch at SW1 (this is best practice but is not enforced)

  • Student must click on the faulty fuse. It will be removed automatically when they click.

  • Student must click on the empty socket to place a new 3A fuse at that location.

  • When the circuit is completed with both of the faulty components replaced, both light bulbs will immediately be lit brightly!

  • Student is able to replace the faulty lead on the board without in-game instructions or feedback.

  • Student is able to recognize that the parallel circuit is now functioning exactly as expected, with the two light bulbs each getting approx 10V of available source power, and both are brightly lit.

  • Note that both bulbs must be equally brightly lit, as they are each getting potential 10V
