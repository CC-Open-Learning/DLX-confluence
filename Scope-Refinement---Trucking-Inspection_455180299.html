Meeting with John on Mon Oct 25 - Notes below this line

Brake chamber, hoses and drums. Cannot be visualized while looking straight at the wheel.

Brake drum - located immediately behind the wheel, could be cracked.

Brake hoses - Same immediate area - abraded - chafe against something to show wear - if leaking you hear a hissing sound

Brake chamber - Same immediate area - cracked, leaking, loose. air brake hoses connect to it and the connection is a common area for damage. Do not touch them. Broken, loose fasteners such as bolts or hose clamps.

Axle seals - leaking

Wheel seals are not visible, but if leaking you would see a spatter of oil radiating outward from behind the wheel. Inside aspect of the wheel and rim - splattered outward by centrifugal force.

Hoses - generally, be on the lookout for hoses that are cracked, loose (clamp failure), damaged. Anywhere, does not need to be its own point in the inspection.

End of notes from Mon Oct 25 meeting

Air Pressure Gauge(s) - Input from John via email 01 NOV 2021:
Inspecting dashboard gauges (in general): You should get in & turn the key to the ON position, but NOT start the engine. All the gauges will make a ‘full sweep’ from 0 to whatever their highest reading is. This USUALLY tells you the gauges are working properly if they make the full sweep. Some (the ones that do not require the engine to be running to read) will settle on their actual reading (like the fuel gauge that does not depend on the engine to be running) You would let them sweep, then start the engine & monitor from there. In our case I would suggest returning them all to 0 anyways as the above doesn’t necessarily serve any purpose here.

Oil pressure gauge: When you start the engine, after a few seconds you should see a valid reading. Oil pressure is probably the most important so I always tell students to watch that one first. If the engine runs for more than about 15 seconds and it doesn’t read it should be shut down asap & further investigating required. Ask me how I know this lol.

Air gauge: When the engine is started, the air pressure gauge will behave as follows:

Air pressure gauge will start at zero and pop to life when the engine is started up.  It will quickly “sweep” and then settle to optimal range within a couple of seconds. The idea is that the truck maintained air pressure while the engine was turned off, so we shouldn't see a really low reading that gradually comes up to optimal with a compressor running.  It should look good pretty much right away, otherwise, that could indicate there is a leak in the system. Note: Compressor is silent, you will not hear it running. From John:

The air compressor runs constantly but silently, it is constantly building and replenishing air. The system pressure will build to approx. 100-120 psi then the blow off valve located on the bottom of the compressor will ‘pop off’ and the excess air will be expelled with a loud clear audible hiss (2-3 seconds of a loud PSSSSSSS’ing noise) dropping the air back down to optimal levels. Usually if the compressor is faulty or there is a significant audible leak, the pressure will only build to a certain level (say 40-50psi) then stabilize there it will not be able to build higher. You would almost never see an air gauge fluctuating, merely reading low although it could be a good teaching tool to include regardless. There are significant air valves within the dashboard and on occasion you will hear the air hissing back out the dashboard from the large red or yellow air buttons. The student will not hear the compressor at any point, just the sound of an air leak located almost anywhere. There is approximately 100 miles of air lines and connections running everywhere so chances are even the best maintained truck in the world will start losing air after being parked for a few hours.

Fuel gauge(s) - There appear to be two external fuel tanks on the truck, one on each side of the tractor vehicle. Do these each have a separate fuel gauge, or are they treated as if they were one big tank? From John: There is one gauge for total fuel capacity, regardless how many external tanks there are.

Fifth Wheel / Coupling mechanism - Does this need to be inspected with a trailer attached? What is the inspection workflow when a trailer is on (because the vehicle in our simulation has a trailer on) From John: We will inspect with the trailer ON and the main point to inspect is that the trailer is securely coupled to the fifth wheel. To verify this, student will inspect that the pin handle and pin indicator are both IN (which indicates that the fifth wheel jaws are CLOSED). We can optionally include a view from under the trailer looking forward toward the cab, at that vantage point the learner can see if the fifth wheel jaws are fully closed around the trailer kingpin to verify that the trailer is securely coupled.

List of items from the Inspection checklist reference material that will NOT be included in this sim:

  • Lift gate: As per John, this is a feature on DZ delivery type trucks that is used for unloading by hand to a ground-level such as a parking lot. This is not a feature that most AZ drivers will ever have, he does not see value in including it in our simulation, it is not appropriate for the model we are using.

  • Dumpbed position, Dumpbed pivot points and end gate - These are specifically for dump trucks and will not be included in our transport truck inspection.

  • Operation of boom - This is for a crane attachment used for mechanical unloading, this will not be included in our transport truck inspection

  • Back up alarm - Do transport trucks have this? Should it be inspected? John: Some do but you cannot inspect it as the truck would have to be running & in reverse gear while you went around the back & listened, same scenario as the reverse lights.

  • Hour log meter - John: some trucks have it but its never relevant so I wouldn’t bother including it.
