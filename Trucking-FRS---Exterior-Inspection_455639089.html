TI-21 - Getting issue details... STATUS

As a student, I need to inspect the overall condition of the exterior of the truck and trailer so that I can identify any damage prior to beginning a trip

User clicks to select damage.

On click: Object outline is highlighted (e.g. bumper, door, or maybe just the damage itself)

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to the specific component selected (e.g. grill, front bumper, or drivers door) so that the user can record appropriate “damaged” state in the inspection checklist.

User is not required to record the absence of exterior damage. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they will click on a “Continue” or “Next Inspection” option that will indicate they are ready to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Options: dented, cracked, abraded

2-4 visible dents or abrasions to the exterior of the cab or the trailer.

Good: No significant damage, just very minor light scuffs or minor dirt.

Dented: 1 or more areas of the vehicle show dents larger than 2cm.

Cracked: 1 or more areas of the vehicle show visible cracks

Abraded: 1 or more areas of the vehicle show abrasions that break the paint and are larger than 1cm

This is separate from window/mirror inspection, even though both types of damage are visible on the exterior. Windows/mirrors will not be selectable in this task.

Fuel tanks

As a student, I need to see the fuel tanks on the exterior of the truck so that I can identify conditions that may make it unsafe to operate my vehicle

User clicks to select any damage or problem with the fuel tanks.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Fuel Tank so that the user can record appropriate “damaged” state in the inspection checklist.

User is not required to record the absence of exterior damage.

Options: damaged, not secure, leaking

0-2 fuel tanks have issues

Good: Fuel tanks are intact, securely attached, and show no damage

Damaged: 1 or more fuel tanks are dented or misshapen.

Not secure: 1 or more fuel tanks show visible signs of being insecurely attached to the truck, such as broken straps, crooked.

Leaking: 1 or more fuel tanks show obvious signs of leakage because the fill cap is misthreaded and crooked. Diesel fuel evaporates very quickly, so the only way to identify a leak is usually by the excessive dust/dirt that sticks to the tank in a defined area where the diesel fuel touched the tank. The dirty area looks fuzzy, almost like velvet, and the dirt wipes easily away because it is sitting on top of the greasy residue from the evaporated diesel.

Fuel leaks do not normally result in a puddle, because diesel fuel evaporates very quickly. Instead of a puddle or dripping liquid, a diesel leak leaves a dark greasy stain behind, which is very sticky. As the truck drives, dirt and dust will stick to the diesel residue, and can get quite thick as more fuel leaks on top of existing dirt/dust, collecting more dirt/dust. The result is a well-defined patch of thick, furry-looking black-brown dirt stuck to the tank in the area of the leak. The rest of the tank will look normal, the dirt will specifically stick to the fuel residue.

Batteries

As a student, I need to see the batteries so that I can identify conditions that may make it unsafe to operate my vehicle

User triggers the evaluation activity, an animated cut scene that demonstrates the opening of the panel on the exterior of the truck, where the DEF tank and batteries are housed.

In the cut scene, the panel will open and the camera will move to the batteries.

If the user observes that the batteries are damaged or sparking/smoking, they will click on the model to report damage in the Inspection UI.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

User may trigger the evaluation activity multiple times to observe the cut scene repeatedly, if desired.

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Batteries so that the user can record appropriate “damaged” state in the inspection checklist.

User is not required to record the absence of exterior damage.

Options: damaged, malfunctioning

0-1 batteries have issues

Batteries are located under a panel which is normally located immediately below the driver’s side door, above/behind the top step. It can also be located along the side of the cab, underneath the sleeper section.

There are 4 batteries arranged in a straight line, wired in parallel. They are rectangular 12V batteries. Each battery has 2 terminals on top. To visually inspect the batteries, the cover must be opened. The cover should automatically close when the student continues to the next step of the inspection.

Good: Batteries show no damage, exterior of battery is intact and shows no signs of swelling or leaking. Terminals are connected securely. No sparks or smoke or blackening is seen.

Damaged: 1 or more batteries are damaged, as follows:

Swollen or misshapen (battery sides are bulging outward, battery does not line up neatly with others)

Leaking (greasy, chalky, yellow-brown residue on exterior of battery, especially at the seams)

Heavily Corroded (battery terminals are caked in amber-brown rusty residue)

Malfunctioning: 1 or more batteries are showing signs of electrical fault, such as blackening of the battery, terminals, cover, or the battery box, in extreme cases this could include smoke or sparks.

See reference video Inspection Video 8 at around the 7-9 minute segment. This shows the access panel for the DEF tank and battery array.

In our video footage, the batteries are covered by a wide plastic cover, but this cover will NOT be included in our simulation, to improve the visibility of the batteries and terminals.

Trailer Doors - Open

As a student, I need to inspect the condition and functionality of the trailer doors so that I can learn to identify conditions that make it unsafe to operate my vehicle

User triggers the evaluation activity, an animated cut scene that demonstrates the opening and closing of the rear trailer doors.

In the cut scene, the trailer doors should start in a closed and secured position. The scene should demonstrate releasing the door closure mechanism and opening the trailer doors to the full extent.

If the user observes that the trailer doors are damaged or not functioning as expected, they will click on the door, door hinge, or door closure mechanism to report damage in the Inspection UI.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

User may trigger the evaluation activity multiple times to observe the cut scene repeatedly, if desired.

If the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to the Trailer Doors component. User records the specific damage/bad state in the inspection checklist.

Options: door damaged, latch damaged, hinge damaged

If the user does not click on the model, they can select the “Next Inspection” option to move to the next inspectable or POI without reporting any damage in the checklist.

0-1 doors show damage or malfunction.

Good: Doors show no visible damage (particularly around the edges, the latches, and hinges). Both doors unlatch smoothly when the handle/mechanism is activated. both doors swing smoothly open on their hinges and can be opened fully.

Door damaged: Either door shows visible dents, abrasions, particularly around the edges.

Latch damaged: This will not occur when the doors are opening, only when they are closing (to avoid interfering with the Cargo Area inspection)

Hinge damaged: One or more hinges is damaged such that the door is unstable and the hinge is visibly damaged. The door must still open to at least 90 degrees to facilitate Cargo Area inspection.

Sound effects can be used to help identify hinge damage, as bent hinges are very noisy.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

User is not required to record conspicuity tape that is present as expected. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Conspicuity tape is present as expected on the cargo van trailer. Occasionally, tape may be damaged (peeling away from the surface of the trailer) or it might be missing completely from a section where it is required.

License plate lights

As a student, I need to inspect the license plate lights so that I can ensure my license plates are visible at night

User walks around the truck and trailer to observe the license plate lights at three required locations: front bumper, rear tractor bumper, rear trailer.

User clicks to select any license plate light that is not in a good state..

On click: Object outline is highlighted

User records inspection results in the inspection checklist to report condition of the license plate lights on the truck.

Options: good, malfunctioning

Additional FAKE bad state: license plate missing

Note that license plate lights are intended to point at the license plate, so that the lamp itself is not usually visible on the truck. Our student will verify that the light is functioning, but will not visualize the light itself.

Infrequently, 1 of the 3 license plate lights will be malfunctioning (does not stay lit, flickers on erratically). This will replace the more traditional “burnt out” state, since it would be very difficult for the user to identify a burned out license plate light while inspecting a truck in broad daylight.

Good: License plate light is on steady and uninterrupted.

Malfunctioning: 1 or more license plate lights is flickering erratically, does not stay lit for more than 1 second at a time.

FAKE BAD STATE License plate missing: This will never be a correct answer, but will be present in the Inspection UI to remind the student that rarely, license plates are stolen from the truck.

This step needs to be done after the step where the “Test Lights” button is activated.
