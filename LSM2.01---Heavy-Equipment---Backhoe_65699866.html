Type of Solution

Procedural - Inspection (Flatscreen)

(PoV) (3D Models & Assets)

Students walk through an inspection of the backhoe required for safety checks before operating. Areas in the inspection will include:

  • Vehicle exterior

  • Cab interior/exterior

  • Engine & Fluid

  • Loader bucket

  • Backhoe end

Objective

The objective of this module is to allow Heavy Equipment student operators to inspect heavy equipment in a virtual setting, as a supplement for practicing with the real machinery. It’s expected that the students would have an understanding of the necessary steps of the pre-op inspection before beginning the simulation, so no new material needs to be presented by the simulation.

As a learning tool, both instant and deferred feedback will be useful. For initial use of the simulation, providing feedback after each step will solidify the student’s understanding of the steps. After becoming familiar, students may use the software as a “practice test” where feedback would be provided after the inspection instead of after each step. This will allow them to asses their own readiness for an in-person summative evaluation.

Success metrics

Goal

Metric

Create an accurate simulation of the components and functions of a Caterpillar 416F backhoe, as needed for the pre-operation inspection.

Assumptions / Known Constraints

  • It is expected that the students be familiar with the vehicle in question.

  • It has been confirmed that although some assets provided aren’t representative of the vehicle being simulated, the overall look and feel between backhoe makes/models are similar.

Pre-Trip Inspection Order

The text below is copied from P2.04 Pre-Trip Inspection. Annotations for developers are added in green.

The order listed below is only a guide to be used as a training-aid along with the operator’s manual that came with the equipment that you will be inspecting.

  1. Start the inspection as you approach the equipment. Look for deflated tires (4k), lowered implements, puddles or fluid leaks, cracked or broken windows, and anything out of the ordinary (1a).
    The user will move around the backhoe using the “on rails” camera to get a 360 view of the the equipment.

  2. Wipe away any dirt so that it does not enter the system. Check all fluids on fluid (2f, 4b), Hydraulic fluid (2e, 4a), Brake fluid (n/a?), Engine coolant (2b), Window-washer fluid (7b?)the equipment as outlined in the operator’s manual they should include: Engine oil (2a), Transmission , and the Fuel-water Separator (2d).
    User will be presented with a list of fluid checks to complete. See Fluid Checks for more details.

  3. If any fluids were found to be below the minimum allowable level, add the appropriate fluid at this point to bring the level above the minimum allowable limit.
    User will be allowed to select and add (the appropriate) fluid based on fluid checks in the prior step.

  4. Enter the cab of the equipment and using the ignition key insert it and turn the ignition switch to the run position without starting the equipment. Allow the equipment monitoring system to cycle through its sensor diagnostic procedure.
    The user can move back around to a door like in step 1 to enter the cab. Click on the ignition key slot to “insert a key and turn it” (fixed animation action). They can then hit the ignition switch which triggers a task check to see if everything is functional.

  5. If no warnings or alarm codes are present, and all gauges appear to be functioning properly, then follow the operator’s manual starting procedure for the current weather conditions and start the engine.
    Quick inspection for warnings/alarms/gauges. Then present the user with the part of the manual for starting procedure based on weather?

  6. After the engine is idling at its proper RPM’s exit the cab, and resume the inspection outside the equipment while allowing the fluids to warm.
    Stall the user inside the cab while the system “reaches proper RPM” (purely cosmetic). User can then leave the cab.

  7. Start with the area directly outside the driver’s door and move towards the front of the equipment. Check the steps, railings, tank or toolbox securement, R.O.P.S, tire inside and outside, steering linkage, axil housing, front drive assembly, lift-arm, pins and retainers, hydraulic cylinders and lines, rocks and debris.
    Guided “on rails” camera setup similar to step 1 to allow the user to for a more in-depth inspection. Inspection setup like in previous modules. Guided as perhaps they click to move to the next stage? Otherwise this and next lot of inspection steps will be on the regular unguided “on rails” camera.

  8. Move to the front loader bucket area and inspect the backside of the bucket and equipment grill area. Look to make sure pins and retainers are present, hydraulic cylinders and lines are not damaged or leaking, look for rocks and debris in recessed areas of the bucket and grill.
    Step 7 continued.

  9. Next look at the actual bucket. Inspect the welds for cracks, the cutting edge for chips or damage, ensure all fasteners for the cutting edge are tight and secure. Check the overall condition of the bucket look for tears or holes in the metal.
    Step 8 continued.

  10. Move to the opposite side of the equipment and begin inspecting the backside of the bucket and grill area. Start with the pins, retainers, cylinders and lines, and look for rocks and debris.
    Step 9 continued.

  11. Move to the front tire area and repeat the process from the other side. Look for cuts and tears in the tire, puddles of fluid on or around the rim, wheel-nuts, steering and axil components.
    Step 10 continued.

  12. Look at the lift-arm, all pins and retainers, hydraulic cylinders and lines, welds for cracks.
    Step 11 continued.

  13. Look at the R.O.P.S including the windows and doors, look at the toolbox or tank securement, steps and hand railings, if available check the battery compartment.
    Step 12 continued.

  14. Move to the rear tire area and repeat the tire inspection. Look at the tire for rips, cuts, and punctures, look for fluid on the inside and outside of the rim, and wheel-nuts for tightness.
    Step 13 continued.

  15. Look at the stabilizer area, inspect the pins and retainers, cylinder and lines, grouser and or pad, rocks and debris.
    Step 14 continued.

  16. Next look at the backhoe area and rear of the cab. Check the R.O.P.S, windows, lights, and puddles under the equipment. Now look at the swing-linkage area, look at the pins and retainers, cylinders and lines, clamps and brackets, rocks and debris.
    Step 15 continued.

  17. Next look at the boom for defects in the metal such as; cracked welds, distorted metal, holes. Look at the cylinder and lines, brackets and clamps, pins and retainers.
    Step 16 continued.

  18. Next move to the stick area and inspect it for metal defects as well as the cylinder and lines, pins and retainers, clamps and brackets.
    Step 17 continued.

  19. Next the backhoe bucket area. Look at the linkage, pins and retainers. Look at the teeth and retainers, bucket metal for holes and cracked welds, rocks and debris.
    Step 18 continued.

  20. Walk down the opposite side of the backhoe towards the cab and inspect this side of the stick and boom area including cylinders, lines, welds, clamps and brackets, metal defects, and rocks and debris
    Step 19 continued.

  21. Check the swing linkage area, stabilizer area, and the rear tire area.
    Step 20 continued.

  22. Battery area, look to make sure the batteries are secure in place, not swollen cracked or damaged, connections are secure and wires not corroded, finally that there are no loose metal objects that could cause a spark or explosion.
    Step 21 continued.

  23. The outside inspection should now be complete including cosmetic damage to the exterior. Move to the cab interior.
    If guided, the previous “move to the next stage” brings them back to the cab interior.

  24. Inside the cab confirm that no alarms or warning have activated while outside the equipment. Inspect the seat for proper securement and adjustability, the brake pedals are locked together and operational, the parking brake, wipers and washer, signal lights, travel alarm, mirrors, operator’s manual, previous days inspection report, steering and gear selector, and any other items listed in the operator’s manual.
    Step 5 inspection repeats essentially. User then inspects the inside of the cab, will be more options/freedom than step 4/5 so perhaps they were locked from be accessible prior to this step. Option for the user to open up relevant parts of the operator’s manual?

  25. Now cycle hydraulics as required and recheck the transmission fluid and any other fluids required by the operator’s manual.
    Repeat step 2 fluid check.

  26. Fill out all required paperwork including equipment hours, location, date, operators name, and any defects or concerns including fluids added.
    User is given the option to make notes/comments based on their inspection.

How guided should the pre-operation inspection be?

If it is being used as a learning/teaching tool, the inspection can be very guided, providing step-by-step instructions for each task in the inspection checklist.

If the approach they are looking for is more of a practice test or self-assessment where the student would be expected to have read some of the material for the inspection beforehand, then the information that we present to the student would tell them what they need to do, but not necessarily how they need to do it.

The objective of this digital simulation is a supplement for practicing with the real machinery, to allow students to get a good feel for it. It’s expected that the students would have an understanding of the necessary steps of the pre-op inspection before beginning the simulation, so no new material needs to be presented by the simulation.

As a learning tool, both instant and deferred feedback will be useful. For initial use of the simulation, providing feedback after each step will solidify the student’s understanding of the steps. After becoming familiar, students may use the software as a “practice test” where feedback would be provided after the inspection instead of after each step. This will allow them to asses their own readiness for an in-person summative evaluation.
