Brake Pedal

As a student, I want to inspect the brake pedal to ensure it offers proper resistance when pressed so that the brakes are in safe condition.

User clicks to trigger the animated cut-scene that demonstrates the brake pedal being pressed.

When triggered, the animation will either show the brake pedal being pressed all the floor with a thump (no resistance), or it will show the brake pedal being pushed down slightly, with a message confirming that the pedal is offering firm pressure as expected.

If everything looks good, the learner will click “Next Inspection” to continue to the next inspectable or next POI.

User clicks on the model to open the Inspection UI and report a bad state.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Brake Pedal so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: no brake pressure

User is not required to record results if the brake pedal is in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Brake pedal offers proper resistance to pressure when pressed.

Good: Brake pedal feels firm but yields to pressure when pressed. It does not go all the way to the floor - it supports the pressure of being pushed downward. It is possible to push the pedal at least halfway down, but it cannot reach the floor. When pressure is released, the pedal returns smoothly to original position.

No brake pressure: Brake pedal is easily pushed all the way to the floor with a thud, and does not spring back up when released.

Loss of pressure in the brake pedal may be too subtle to easily convey in the virtual inspection. We may need an additional prompt (toast message or voice over) to help the student identify that the brake pedal is not behaving normally.

Note that the student must have their foot on the brake pedal to start the engine, this is true even when the brake pedal is not operating correctly.

User clicks on the model to open the Inspection UI and report a bad state.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

Gear shift is rarely left in gear.

Left in gear: Gear shifter is in any other gear (not neutral), indicating that the truck was left in gear and it is not safe to start the engine.

At the end of the “Left in gear” state, the gear shift will need to be put in neutral, or else the truck cannot be started. The student will put the truck into neutral gear before starting the engine.

Steering Wheel

As a student, I want to inspect the steering wheel for damage, to ensure that the steering mechanism is in safe condition.

User clicks to trigger the animated cut-scene that demonstrates the steering wheel being moved to evaluate the “play” or “looseness” of the wheel.

When triggered, the animation will show the steering wheel being turned to the right and left, as though steering the truck around obstacles. The steering wheel should move easily, but the degree of free movement determines if the condition is good or bad. See detailed explanation in States column.

If everything looks good, the learner will click “Next Inspection” to continue to the next inspectable or next POI.

User clicks on the model to open the Inspection UI and report a bad state.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Steering Wheel so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: loose

User is not required to record results if the steering wheel is in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Steering wheel is centred. Truck steer tires are pointing straight.

Good: Steering wheel can be moved easily to the right or left a VERY SMALL distance, e.g. 10cm. Beyond this point, it would be necessary to put much more effort into turning the wheel, and the steer tires would turn to the right or left when you do so.

Excessive play: Steering wheel can be easily moved to the right or the left a LARGER distance, such as 20cm or approx 1/4 of the total circumference. Note that the movement of the steering wheel is easy, does not require much effort to turn, and the steer tires of the truck DO NOT MOVE when the steering wheel moves. This condition is called excessive “play” and indicates a potentially serious maintenance issue in the steering linkages.

Excessive “play” in the steering wheel is caused by mechanical failure or maintenance issue in the steering mechanisms attaching the steering wheel to the steer tires of the cab. The learner does not need to identify the exact problem, but they need to identify if there is too much “play” in the wheel, which is evidenced by how far you can easily turn the wheel without turning the tires.

It is understood that the learner cannot see the steer tires from the cab interior, so we may need to tinker with the exact right way to show this problem. The fact is that you don’t need to actually see the tires to determine if they are turning, because turning the tires while the truck is parked requires quite a lot of effort. We can focus on how far the wheel can be turned EASILY without any resistance, and that will likely be sufficient vs actually getting out to look at the steer tires.

Parking Brakes (Truck and Trailer)

As a student, I want to ensure that the truck and trailer parking brakes are applied so that I can safely conduct my exterior inspection.

UPDATED FEB 2 per content expert:

Student verifies that the truck and trailer parking brake controls on the dashboard are activated. These are two large push/pull buttons on the dashboard, red and yellow plastic (see reference photos for specific appearance). Brakes are activated when the button is pulled fully out from the dashboard. The student must NOT release and reapply the parking brakes to inspect them. They simply verify that the buttons are pulled all the way out as far as they can go.

This is not an inspection, but we will use a physical inspection button to trigger the animation. Checking the parking brakes is a required step to proceed with the inspection. The learner will follow the prompts to verify the brakes are activated (or will activate them if they were not applied) and then click “Next Inspection” to continue to the next inspectable or next POI.

User clicks on the physical inspection button to trigger an animation where a hand/glove pulls each one of the buttons (Parking Brake and Trailer Air Supply) buttons outward, away from the dashboard.

As a tactile “inspection” step, a toast message will be shown at the top of the screen to tell the user how the buttons “feel”.

If the user clicks on the model, there will be no Inspection panel with bad states to select. This is not an inspection.

Very rarely, the truck or trailer parking brake will not be activated, the truck was left in an unsafe condition.

This is not an inspection - the parking brakes will ALWAYS work and there will be no Inspection Checklist. This is because a failure of the parking brake would mean that it would be unsafe to complete the exterior inspection, and we don’t want that to happen in our sim.

Instead, we will show two possibilities to help illustrate to the student how essential it is to always manually check that the parking brakes are securely applied:

Good: Animated hand/glove pulls each of the two buttons outward from the dashboard. The buttons do not move. Toast message indicates that the brakes are securely applied.

Bad: Animated hand/glove pulls each of the two buttons outward from the dashboard. One or both buttons show that they are being pulled outward. Toast message indicates that the brakes were NOT fully applied, and assure that the brakes are now secured.

The student MUST verify that the parking brakes are applied before conducting the exterior inspection, particularly the wheel inspections. This inspection must be done MANUALLY, a visual inspection is NOT ADEQUATE. If the parking brake(s) are not applied for either the truck or the trailer, the student must ensure they are applied before proceeding with the inspection.

Windshield wipers and washer

As a student, I want to inspect the windshield wipers and washer, to ensure that I can maintain good visibility through the windshield while operating the truck.

User clicks to trigger the animated cut-scene that demonstrates the windshield wipers and washer being activated.

When triggered, the animation will show the windshield wipers being activated using the dashboard controls. The windshield washer must be sprayed as well.

If everything looks good, the learner will click “Next Inspection” to continue to the next inspectable or next POI.

User clicks on the model to open the Inspection UI and report a bad state.

On click: Object outline is highlighted

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Windsheild Wipers and Washer so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: damaged, missing, wiper malfunction, washer malfunction

User is not required to record results if the windshield wipers and washer are in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

0-1 Windshield wipers are missing, damaged, or malfunctioning.

0-1 Windshield washer sprayer jets do not spray when activated.

Good: 2 windshield wipers are present, and they move as expected when activated. Washer fluid is sprayed onto the correct area of the windshield when activated. Wipers clear the washer fluid from the windshield after it is sprayed.

Damaged: 1 or 2 wipers are bent

Missing: 1 wiper is missing (either the entire arm or just the blade portion).

Wiper malfunction: 1 or 2 wipers do not move as expected when activated, or fail to clear washer fluid from the windshield when activated.

Washer malfunction: 1 or 2 washer jets do not spray washer fluid onto the windshield when activated.

It is required by MTO that both the wipers and the washer fluid spray are inspected and functional.

Note: This is not a problem where the washer fluid reservoir is low. This inspection is focused on the functionality of the windshield washer sprayer mechanism. Fluid levels will be checked separately in Release III.

Gauges

As a student I want to inspect the gauges on the dashboard so that I can determine if they move, light up, and present correct readings.

User must locate and visually inspect the gauges on the dashboard. Some specific gauges will be inspected individually, but this story covers all gauges not specifically named elsewhere.

If the student observes a problem that needs to be recorded on the checklist, they will click on the dashboard.

On click, the gauge is highlighted.

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Gauges so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: malfunctioning, damaged

User is not required to record results if the dashboard gauges are in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

0-2 gauges are not functioning as expected.

Good: Functional gauges should “sweep” when the key is turned to the ON position. This means that the gauges start from zero and will quickly “sweep” to the furthest extent of the gauge and return to settle on a realistic reading.

Malfunctioning: Malfunctioning gauges do not move at all when the key is turned, or show wildly fluctuating or wildly inaccurate readings, or fail to light up.

Damaged: Broken gauges may be missing the indicator needle or the needle could be broken (pointing away from the gauge range)

Most gauges will show no reading while the key is not in the truck. When the key is turned to the “ON” position, gauges will “sweep” and some gauges will give a valid reading (e.g. fuel gauge) even without the engine running. It is not actually universal for gauges to “sweep” some will just show a reading right away, but John prefers for us to show gauges that sweep so that students can recognize that as a way of checking that gauges are functional.

Key turned halfway: All analogue gauges “sweep” and return to zero. Voltmeter shows 12-12.8V.

Engine started: Gauges do NOT sweep again.

Fuel and DEF gauges rise quickly to their correct value and then don’t move.

Air pressure and oil pressure gauges rise SLOWLY to their correct value. NOTE that air pressure and oil pressure gauges will take between 30-60 seconds to give a proper reading - this is why we don’t have the student read them immediately after starting the engine!

Speedometer stays as 0 for the entire sim, because the truck does not move.

Tachometer is at 0 until the engine is started, then it reads somewhere around 900-1200RPM steady for the rest of the sim.

Voltmeter goes from 12.XV up to 14.4V when the engine is started.

See the video Inspection 15 around 2min in for great close-up footage of the dashboard when the gauges are initialized. You will see the warning lights all come on at once and notice that those gauges do not actually do a full sweep (but you get a good idea of the movement of the needle and imagine a gauge that would do a full sweep before giving a reading)

Oil Pressure

As a student I want to inspect the oil pressure gauge so that I can learn to identify important vehicle maintenance conditions.

User must locate and visually inspect the oil pressure gauge on the dashboard. Oil pressure is the FIRST thing a student should check after starting the engine (within 10-15 seconds)

If the student observes a problem that needs to be recorded on the checklist, they will click on the oil pressure gauge.

On click, the oil pressure gauge is highlighted.

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Oil Pressure so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: low pressure, malfunctioning

User is not required to record results if the oil pressure is in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Very rarely, oil pressure is very low, or the gauge is malfuntioning

Good: Oil pressure reading is stable and in expected range.

Low: Oil pressure gauge shows very low pressure

Malfunctioning: Oil pressure gauge is fluctuating or needle is missing

John explained that low oil pressure is 0-10 PSI. There is no such thing as high oil pressure.

Because oil pressure is so critical to the engine running, we will make two considerations:

  1. Oil pressure gauge must not be malfunctioning. It will always give a valid reading. (Good or Low pressure)

  2. Oil pressure gauge must be read within 30-60 seconds of starting the engine. The engine must be running for at least 30 seconds to get a valid reading, but the truck must NOT be left idling if it has no oil. It should be inspected about 30-60 sec after starting the engine but BEFORE completing the exterior inspection of the truck.

Air gauges

As a student I want to inspect the air gauges so that I can learn to maintain my vehicle in a safe operating condition.

User must locate and visually inspect the air gauges on the dashboard.

If the student observes a problem that needs to be recorded on the checklist, they will click on the air gauge.

On click, the air gauge is highlighted.

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Air so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: low, high, gauge malfunction

User is not required to record results if the air compressor system is in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Uncommonly, air pressure is low.

Very rarely, the gauge is malfuntioning

Good: Air gauges show stable pressure in optimal range.

Low pressure: 1 or more air gauges show low pressure between 10-50 PSI, The needle will keep moving, it will not be stable, because the pressure cannot be maintained if there is a leak.

High pressure: 1 or both gauges read 125 PSI or higher.

Gauge malfunction: 1 or more air gauges are fluctuating wildly or needle is missing

There are often multiple air pressure gauges on the dash because there are primary are secondary air tanks. Air 1 will show higher than Air 2 while filling, but will be equal when filled.

The tanks are separate. If one is leaking, they are not both leaking - only the affected tank will show low. However, if the compressor is not functioning, both tanks will read low. If the compressor is not shutting off when it should, both tanks will read high.

Air pressure gauges start as zero and “sweep” when the key is turned halfway, they will settle back to zero.

The air compressor system will automatically come on when the engine is started and will run automatically as needed to maintain pressure. Low pressure means that there is a serious leak or the compressor is not functioning properly. High pressure means that the compressor is not stopping when optimal pressure is reached.

Volt meter

As a student I want to inspect the volt meter so that I can learn to ensure my battery is in a safe operating condition.

User must locate and visually inspect the volt meter on the dashboard. This gauge shows that the truck battery is in good condition and is holding a charge.

If the student observes a problem that needs to be recorded on the checklist, they will click on the volt meter on the dashboard.

On click, the gauge is highlighted.

When the user clicks on the model, the Inspection UI opens to Volt Meter so that the user can record appropriate “bad” state in the inspection checklist.

Options: low voltage

User is not required to record results if the volt meter and battery are in good condition. If the user does not click on the model to report damage, they may click the Next Inspection option to move on to the next inspectable or POI.

Uncommonly, volt meter will show that the battery is quite low.

Good: Volt meter shows at least 12.2 volts when the key is turned halfway. This reading will go to at least 14 or 14.2 when the engine is started and the alternator is running.

Low: Volt meter reads less than 12 volts at any time.

The volt meter on our model is digital, so the gauge does not “sweep” and the gauge does not malfunction.

NOTE: The voltmeter must be read within 30-60 seconds of starting the engine, to ensure that adequate voltage is present to run the truck. It should be inspected about 30-60 sec after starting the engine but BEFORE completing the exterior inspection of the truck.
