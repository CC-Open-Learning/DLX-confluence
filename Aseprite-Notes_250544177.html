Aseprite Documentation 

 

Notes and Information Regarding Aseprite 

 

 
During our time working for the VAR Lab, our team has been tasked with the job of building an JRPG surrounding the student experience at Conestoga College. Since our team was using RPG Maker MZ, the software of choice for sprite creation was Aseprite, since it’s optimized for pixel art. During our short time using Aseprite, we have learned the necessary methods for creating, exporting, and implementing sprite sheets using this software. 

 

Tip: 

Additional Info: 

Create a new project by selecting the “New” option from the file dropdown menu. You could also press CTRL+N to start a new canvas. 

 

When making an asset, keep your canvas size to 48x48 so it fits seamlessly with RPG Maker MZ’s existing tile sheets.  

48x48 is the minimum accepted by RPG Maker MZ, but you may use sprites that are bigger in size - though they will not mesh well with the existing assets that RPG Maker MZ provides. 

 

 

Even though multiple people may work on the same sheets, they ultimately have to be combined into one before being implemented into RPG Maker MZ. 

One person is sent all of the Aseprite files (we sent them through teams) to combine into the sprite sheets via Aseprite on a new canvas. 

Sheets are specific to the type of sprites associated with it. Basically, keep all your floor tiles together on one sheet, your objects on another, and so on. 

 

Once you have your desired sprites finalized, you can create a sprite sheet by making a new canvas and placing your designs on it. 

It’s important to make your sprite sheets in multiples of your asset pixel size. For example, if your designs are 48x48, you’d want to make it something like 768x768 or 480x480. This will ensure that the assets are evenly spaced out and centered within each tile.  

When making a sprite sheet, the first spot on the top left of the sprite sheet should be blank. 

This is due to RPG Maker MZ needing an empty tile for blank spaces (its default is the top-left). 

After finalizing your sprite sheet, you can press file at the top and select export to export the file you’re working on. 

 

The toolbar is located on the right-most side of the canvas.This contains the tools necessary to make your pixel art! 

Most of these tools have secondary tools associated with them if you left-click on them. 

 

 

Press SHIFT+N to create a new layer, or just right click on your existing layer and select “New Layer” from the popup menu. This menu is where you can find things like duplicate and flatten. 

This menu also contains important functions, as well as the properties menu. 

 

 

In the properties menu, you can edit the layer name, change the “mode” of the layer, as well as change the layer opacity 

 

To change your brush’s opacity, refer to the opacity bar below the colour wheel. 

Any opacity settings selected will be reverted when selecting a new colour. 

 

 

Holding CTRL whilst scrolling the wheel on your mouse changes your brush size. This works with the eraser tool as well. 

Scroll downward to increase the size, and upward to decrease the size. 

When holding ALT, left-clicking will apply the colour to the left click, whilst right-clicking will assign a colour to the right click (so you can have two colours going at once). 

 

When the eraser tool is selected and active, the left-click will utilize the basic eraser function, while the right click works as a colour-replacement tool. 

The colour replace works by replacing the left-click colour with the right-click colour. For example, if you wished to replace every blue pixel with pink, you would hold ALT (while using the eraser tool) and left-click the blue, and then right-click the pink. Then, let go of the ALT key, and start replacing the desired colours by holding down right-click and drawing with the eraser. 

The Magic wand tool allows the user to select every pixel containing the colour selected within the confines of the area (when continuous is turned on). 

With the continuous button turned off, the Magic Wand tool will select every pixel within the entire canvas that matches the selected colour. 

Holding shift allows the user to make multiple selections at once, and CTRL+D deselects everything the user has selected. 

 

 

Holding ALT whilst scrolling the wheel scrolls along the colour wheel. 

This will change your primary colour (the left-click colour) 

Double clicking the layer’s name will prompt you to rename it. 

Make sure you name them appropriately so they’re easier to find. 

Whilst drawing, if you press the opposite click than the one you started with, it will cancel the line before it’s been finalized. 

If you’re drawing with the left click and bump the right click, it will cancel the line (and vice versa). This can be both useful and annoying. 

One trick for creating a straight line is to hover over the spot you want the line to end, and hold down Shift. The line won’t be drawn until you click. 

Another method is to use the line tool from the toolbar, which is really easy to use! The shortcut for that is L. 

 

 

CTRL+T is the transform function, allowing users to resize selected work. 

Resizing is only locked on an axis if hitting shift AFTER clicking the corner.  

Clicking the lock icon on the left of the layer locks the user from making changes on said layer. This menu also has a button to hide said layer. 

 

 

 

The following are notes provided by our artists regarding our tile specifications: 

 

Notes about handling the RPG Maker sprite files:  

  • Everything is on three different sheets  

    • One for Floor  

    • One for Walls  

    • One for Objects  

  • Even though multiple people work on the same sheets, they ultimately have to be combined into one.  

  • One person is sent all the files to combine into the sprite sheets via aseprite.  

  • Once done, we send the sprite sheets to the RPG Maker guys and they handle the rest from there.  

  

Notes about creating Floor Tiles:  

  • The sprite sheets are 768x576 pixels large  

  • Each individual floor tiles will take up a 96 x 144 area on your grid on your sprite sheet.  

  • Each floor tile consists of three parts:  

    • Two smaller 48x48 tiles  

    • One larger 96x96 tile  

  • The two smaller tiles are very important!  

    • The leftmost smaller tile is for a 1x1 tile on rpg maker, it's for an individual tile by itself.  

    • The rightmost smaller tile is the tile created for the center of a + shape.  

  • The larger tile essentially consists of 9 smaller tiles. They consist of the different edges you would use as well as the center tiles. Make sure the tiling makes sense between all parts!  

    • I'd recommend making a tileable center tile first, copying it to all 9 parts of this larger tile before working on the border of the tile.  

  

Notes about creating the Wall Sprite Sheet  

  • The sprite sheets are 768x768 pixels large  

  • Each wall tile consists of four parts:  

    • Two smaller 48x48 tiles  

    • Two larger 96x96 tiles  

  • The smaller tiles are the same idea as the floor tiles  

  • The two larger tiles work the same as the floor tiles, except that the top tile is designed for the ceiling, and the bottom one is meant for the wall.   

  

Notes about creating the Object Sprite Sheet  

  • The sprite sheets are 768x768 pixels large  

  • Each individual tile in RPG Maker is 48x48, so keep that in mind while spriting.  

    • You can make larger objects than this, but keep in mind that it will take up more tiles in RPG maker. Only do this if the object is designed to be particularly large in the first place.  

  • RPG maker is finicky, and needs a transparent area in the top left area of the sheet. Specifically, you should leave an area of about 48 across by 96 down blank while spriting. This is used for deleting objects in RPG maker.  

  • While spriting, you should keep in mind where in the 48x48 area your sprite should be placed. For example, if it's meant to be propped up against a northern wall, align your sprite so that it's pressed against the top of the 48x48 area.  

  • Scaling is very finicky with sprites, and unless scaled in even multiples (x2, x3, etc) it's not usually recommended.   

    • If you do scale something in this way, fixing up imperfections is a must.  

    • Scaling up text to take up more tile spaces is recommended over spriting across multiple tiles for a single character, this is because scaling up one tile to take up 4 or 9 tiles is scaling up at a whole number multiplier. Adding more detail after upscaling can be beneficial, but not necessary.
