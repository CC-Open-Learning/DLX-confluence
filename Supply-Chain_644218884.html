March 6th, 2024 (Proposal meeting)

  • Two DLX proposals were shared as well as one VConestoga digital environment. 

  • The first Proposal focuses on the roles within a warehouse and the learner would go through each one-by-one. 

  • The second proposal puts the learner in an observational position in a warehouse from a top-down view. They interact with different elements and see the results of that. 

  • The VConestoga idea would be to create a virtual warehouse that multiple students could visit at a time and experience videos. 

  • The two proposals were received well by the Content Expert, with special emphasis on management sim.  

    • It’s the most similar to what they teach in class.

  • Major learning outcome is system thinking, looking at all of the systems and how they interact with each other. 

  • Learner will be empathetic to the jobs involved. 

  • There needs to be waste, problems, issues that need to be solved for the learner to address. 

  • Highlight different sections in the warehouse. 

  • For first release it could have no variation, just a repeatable pattern. 

  • The ideal spot for release is September but it can be delayed to January. 

  • Events can be delayed until a second release. 

  • Following a box. 

  • Model for problems. 

  • Size and weight changes the way the operation works. 

  • A VConestoga sim isn’t entirely what they’re looking for. 

  • Allowing learner to make decisions and then get feedback on those decisions. 

February 20, 2024

  • Description of the different roles within a warehouse environment. 

    • Receiver

    • Material Handler 

    • Picker 

    • Packer 

    • Shipper 

    • Cycle counter 

  • In real life, the same person can fulfill multiple roles but for the purposes of the DLX each role should be distinct. 

  • Cross-Docking is an improvement on general warehouse design. It’s a system in which a material handler removes materials from a truck and are immediately placed on a conveyor removing the “messy humans” from the equation. 

  • This should not be featured in the DLX, the idea is that the students will understand that it would be a major improvement in efficiency 

  • Every supply chain have returns but the idea in the sim was brushed aside for a September release date. 

  •  Video showing picker was shared and will be a helpful reference going forward with the sim. 

  • Getting industry partners in real life is very difficult. 

  • The work that a student does with an industry partner is to see employees interface with the warehouse and report back. 

  • Represent multiple humans in the warehouse, they don’t need to be realistic. 

  • Interfacing with the humans is not necessary. 

  • Narrative can be provided outside of the sim (Econestoga) 

  • Learner observation, timer, keeping track of machinery, material handling, fork trucks etc. 

  • Empty boxes, empty pallets 

  • Storage is wasted money (crossdocking helps with this) 

  • Depending on the size of the warehouse, there would be multiple people which have the same role. 

  • “What type of warehouse” the size and work needed will determine the equipment. Some warehouses have temperature regulation. 

  • Ultimate vision includes interactivity with the warehouse. 

February 5th, 2024

  • Begin with talk of scalability of the project.

    • CE wants to make sure this is something the VARLab will build upon and enhance in the future rather than something that gets built and never touched again.

  • Supply Chain has multiple areas of focus, manufacturing distribution center, warehouse, engineering, planning, purchasing etc.

    • The idea is to pick one to start with, a warehouse environment

    • Build upon DLX to fit other programs or create different sims for different programs.

  • “Creator Tools” could be implemented to allow a faculty member to customize a scenario.

    • “Creator Tools” is still an experimental idea and not a complete thing.

  • Students observe an operation and view data (inventory, time etc.)

  • Supply Chain is flow, it’s important to be able to see the flow.

    • Secondary goal would be to get the learner orientated in the operational environment.

    • Where is the flow? What is the direction, what’s the volume, what’s the flow rate? Where is it being disrupted?

    • VSM (Value Stream Mapping)

  • Students view or select a sequence collecting data and then talking with humans.

  • The human component can be outside of the scope of the sim, however the idea of “messy humans” making mistakes is integral to show happening in the process.

  • The students are in a 3 year program and this will be related to their capstone or terminus.

  • Normally they’d bring their toolbox with them and observe a real warehouse and determine which tool works best in which situation/

  • Creating the DLX with the assumption they’ve been taught the material already.

  • What the program does now is recruiting industry partners and students go in-person to perform the work.

    • Unsustainable.

    • Relies on the maturity of students. They’ve been surprised and disappointed with how students acted in previous terms.

    • Finding fewer and fewer industry partners which is unfair to students.

  • Warehouse environment, all students receive same data or randomized data, look at situations and come up with different solutions.

  • Highest priority is figuring out the flow and taking that data with them.

    • Afterward, instructors can help guide them as they know what’s in the DLX.

  • Collaboration, room for students to collaborate on the same project.

  • Ideal milestone has something deliverable by September 2024.

“We wanted to update you on the discussions we’ve been having with Stephen and the steering committee we’ve set up for the development of a Supply Chain simulation. One of the things that came out in our conversations is that this group is already successfully using a couple of simulations for developing critical thinking in supply chain management – in fact, it’s one of the most interesting and effective program-wide adaptations of simulations we’ve seen yet at the college. We’d like to feature the impressive work your team has been doing with simulations and the teaching strategies they use as a way of sharing exemplary work in this area.

There is an area we still think we can make a real value-add in this program. In the capstone course, students go into industry and analyze a manufacturing process in the business. This is an extremely popular and effective course, but it is resource intensive and requires substantial investment by faculty members. Although it is a great learning opportunity, it is difficult to scale and sustain. With growing registration numbers, this is an area that a simulation could create a powerful learning experience. Creating a digital twin of a factory environment would open up a world of options, including value stream mapping, time studies, process alignment analysis, etc. We can see this as being a real differentiator. This simulation could be utilized in diploma, degree, and post-graduate levels of supply chain programs.”

DLX Pre-Flight Pillars

Concept & Learning Objectives

No agreement yet on what this simulation might look like or what the scope might be. Several ideas have been brainstormed, including a full “digital” twin of a manufacturing facility, for immersive headset VR, where the instructors could customize and manipulate everything from the type of objects being manufactured to the batch rate. Some discussion about having a player walk around multiple rooms or areas of the virtual facility to locate a target object, then lift their headset visor to have a real-world interaction with a piece of equipment (not in VR) and then lower the visor to resume VR play. The vision is very broad and exploring possibilities at this stage.

At the concept level, this project appears to be a virtual workplace (manufacturing) environment where the learner will practice various activities that might range from compliance inspection to a choose-your-adventure style case study. Note that there is nothing about this DLX so far that could not work equally well on a flat screen, so we have options for this design (depending on what stakeholders decide they need).

Stated by CE Stephen Thomson in his email of July 15, 2022:
”I wanted to share with you a sample of the type of case study that we are looking to adapt from ATS.  The learning objective is to familiarize participants with the ATS Business Model (ABM) the stuctured problem solving methodology utilized by ATS.  In terms of Conestoga students, the important aspect is exposure and practice using a structured approach.”

Sample case study scenario and forms used in following the problem-solving process.

Remaining questions:

  1. There was some discussion about the case study details being customizable by the instructor - what would that be like? How many different variables will be used and in how many combinations?

  2. What kind of feedback or guidance should the learner have?

  3. How hands-on will the problem solving process be? Is it important for the learner to practice job skills such as data input, measuring, weighing, scanning, printing in a hands-on way, or should the learner actions be more focused on choosing the actions (decision-making vs hands-on skill)?

Program Buy-In
