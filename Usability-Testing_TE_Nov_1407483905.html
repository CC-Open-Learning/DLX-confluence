Findings

  • Participants: 30 students paired into groups of 2 or 3; 17 survey responses received.

  • Device Use: All students used trackpads instead of a mouse.

  • Technical Performance: Simulation crashed on one system - 3 times

  • Time to complete the tasks: ranged between 20 mins to 40 mins

POSITIVE

  • Realism and Practical Application:

    • Mimicked in-field work and real-life lab processes.

    • Helped students visualize and plan tasks effectively before labs.

    • Provided an interactive experience for learning.

  • Ease of Use and Feedback:

    • Clear layout of the menu

    • Offered code references for errors.

    • Allowed modifications and adjustments during tasks before asking for feedback

    • Provided the location of where the error was (Select and mount boxes task)

System specs on which the sim crashed

System specs on which the sim worked

Sim crashed on one system-crashed 5 minutes and 30 minutes in at supervisor feedback

CE observed rapid clicking from these team of students

  • Save & load Preferences: 10 students preferred - a prompt to resume at the last task but open to the menu they previously were working on.

Improvement Suggestions

  • Tutorial & Orientation:

    • Students requested a tutorial or introductory narrative outlining expected tasks and objectives.

    • Many students expressed difficulty in orienting themselves in the space, and several suggested enhanced navigation options to help them better understand object locations and task flow.

  • Interactive Features:

    • Desired improved feedback on task completion, such as a progress bar and better task confirmation notifications.

    • Students thought the entries in the clipboard are a link - preferred interactive supervisor notes where each listed error/problem could act as a link that navigates to the specific issue in the simulation.

  • Improved Engagement and Decision-making:

    • Introduce a budget-based system where every decision impacts the available resources, time, or cost. This incentivizes students to make well-informed decisions with practical outcomes.

    • Troubleshooting- Set up scenarios where students troubleshoot issues at a job site—like a home with an electrical fault or a call job where they diagnose and resolve the issue.

    • Roleplay mode TURN OFF SUPERVISOR FEEDBACK - where one student acts as an electrician performing a task, while the other assumes the role of an inspector ensuring safety, compliance, and efficiency.

 Research Goals and Objectives

Research Goals

Research Questions

Answers

Ease of use & Navigation (User Experience)

Find out how do students navigate using the DLX in order to complete tasks. How successful are they and how do they go about completing tasks?

Can the students navigate independently? Is it intuitive to move around and how do you find the speed of movement?

 Some users preferred to navigate around in the space to get oriented and familiarized with the floor plan layout.

With what ease or difficulty are the students able to complete the levels in the DLX? Where do they get stuck?

 Allows users to complete tasks in a sequential manner. The barrier discovered was the traversing to complete a task in the sub levels which can be avoided by opening the task menu from where they left off.

Promotes learning (Instructional Design)

 Discover the aspects of design that support their learning achieved from the DLX

Are the students able to understand what is needed to be done to achieve the learning outcomes?

 Students preferred a tutorial to understand the purpose of the DLX

How would they use this resource? How would this help them with their learning?

What would be the frequency at which they would use this DLX? which parts of it are helpful?

 To practice their knowledge in understanding the electrical codebook application that is being referred through codes in the DLX. It helps in recall and recognition, so the frequency of usage is dependent on the user’s comfort in the practical application.

Engagement Factor (Game Design)

 Understand how users stay engaged to complete an inspection while also determining the pain points encountered.

Do they understand the controls that are being used in the DLX? Do they have any pain points?

 Other than the bugs which were previously detected i.e. the supervisor feedback was not getting updated, the familiarity in the first few tasks made them confident in expecting what the consecutive tasks would entail.

 

What would keep them motivated to play?
