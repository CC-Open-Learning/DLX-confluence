Release Notes - 28 April 2023

Modules and Features

This DLX consists of 4 separate modules, which can be done in any order or any combination. While we recommend that the more general Shop Safety Orientation module be completed first, it is not strictly required, as the content of each module can be used independently from the others.

  1. Shop Safety Orientation - The main focus of this module is essential daily safety practices that students must understand before attending classes in the shop. This module covers all the basics of being safe and ready to work in the shop, including what wear (and what NOT to wear), required PPE, and the importance of being fit for duty.

  2. Lathe Machine Safety Orientation - This module focuses on general orientation to the lathe, including the main parts of the machine, how to operate the emergency stop, and some main hazards to be aware of before starting up or operating the machine. This module does NOT include any learning objectives related to the proper use or workflows for the machine, this is only intended to cover preliminary safety information for a student that is new to the lathe.

  3. Milling Machine Safety Orientation - This module focuses on general orientation to the milling machine, including the main parts of the machine, how to operate the emergency stop, and some main hazards to be aware of before starting up or operating the machine. This module does NOT include any learning objectives related to the proper use or workflows for the machine, this is only intended to cover preliminary safety information for a student that is new to the milling machine.

  4. Surface Grinder Machine Safety Orientation - This module focuses on general orientation to the surface grinder, including the main parts of the machine, how to operate the emergency stop, and some main hazards to be aware of before starting up or operating the machine. This module does NOT include any learning objectives related to the proper use or workflows for the machine, this is only intended to cover preliminary safety information for a student that is new to the surface grinder.

  5. Minigames to Practice Skills - Students will play short minigames in each module to help them practice what they have learned. These games can be fun and even a bit silly, but they are a proven way to help make sure that students can apply information that is covered in each learning topic.

  6. Knowledge Checks and Instant Feedback - Students will be required to answer short questions to demonstrate what they have understood, and will receive immediate feedback about each answer. Knowledge checks help to keep the learner engaged and provide them with a way to demonstrate that they really understand the material.

  7. Save and Load - This feature will help students keep track of where they left off, if they need to exit a module without completing it. Tasks that were in-progress will be restarted from the beginning, but a student who needs to exit and return later will not lose any significant progress.

Objectives and Deliverables

The objective of this module is to enhance learning outcomes for students in the Precision Machining program, specifically related to shop orientation and shop safety.

As a teaching and learning tool, the DLS will focus on a 3-stage user experience that includes a high level of user interaction.

EXPLAIN

SHOW

DO

Tell me what needs to be done and why.

Show me what to do and how to do it

Let me do it myself to practice what I’ve learned

As in all DLS outcomes, we need to strictly avoid any feedback or results that could be mistaken by the learner as a “grade”, as these learning experiences are not being graded.

This project is planned to be the first DLS to be built on COREv2, specifically for use of the visual graph editor.

The completed DLS is planned to deliver 4 learning modules, which can be played in any order and must retain their learning value when played individually or as a set.

Shop Safety Orientation - The focus of this module will be to cover those general shop safety orientation topics that are common across different types of shops: What to wear, PPE, standard safety practices and protocols for a safe workplace. There are no plans for this DLS to delve into specifics about OSHA, WHMIS, Fire Safety, First Aid, or other specific topics. The vision is for this module to be a foundation upon which future topics could be layered as needed.

Machine Safety Orientation - This set of modules will each cover the same orientation protocol, for a different machine. This DLS project is planned for 3 machine orientation modules: Lathe, Milling Machine, Surface Grinder. There are no plans to include other machines as part of the Precision Machining DLS, but the collection may be expanded in the future as the program continues to evolve.

Timeline for Deployment

Delivery target for this DLS has been very roughly estimated as W23 (meaning anytime within the Winter 2023 term, we expect to complete development). This delivery date cannot be estimated with any greater precision until the following things have happened:

  • We arrive at an agreement with project stakeholders regarding scope and design.

  • We obtain the required photo/video assets required to implement the agreed scope.

Assumptions / Known Constraints

  • It is expected that the DLS will be used as an enhancement for learning about shop safety, not as a replacement for in-person learning experiences.

  • We will not cover any specific learning objectives for OSHA, WHMIS, or Fire Safety in this DLS.

  • Photos of the shop environment cannot be taken until September 2022, when they move into the new Reuters facility. Until Sept 2022, we will need to rely on placeholder images. We can take equipment photos or procedural videos as needed before Sept 2022, as long as we minimize the background shop environment and focus specifically on the machine or the worker demonstrating a procedure.

  • There is no 3D art planned for this DLS whatsoever.

Functional Requirements

The following child pages describe the functional requirements for the Precision Machining DLS. Taken from the Project Charter June 1, 2022:

THIS SECTION IS NOT YET READY FOR USE - CONTENT AND SCOPE ARE NOT YET DECIDED

Many of the students in these programs are international students who have never set foot in a machine shop before; this simulation is meant to give them genuine insight into what that experience might be like, and the expectations of the equipment and knowledge they need before they start classes.

The key outcomes for this project will be for students to be able to:

  • Identify the workplace hazards inherent in shops to work safely. The intended students this is intended to augment instruction for would include: mechanical technicians, general machinists and mechanical technician tool and dye makers (all level 1).

  • There are three key machines used in the shop: The lathe machine, the milling machine, and the grinder/surface grinder. Students are currently required to attend a pre-startup safety class in which they view a demonstration, go through a checklist, answer a few questions and they and the instructor sign a form stating they know how to operate the machinery safely.

    • There are several hazards students must be aware of to safely operate the machinery in the shop – for example, leaving the chuck key in the chuck can injure the operator, wearing clothing with long sleeves or strings can result in severe injury if it gets caught in the machinery.

    • Students must be aware of how to safely handle sharp tools

    • and of the dangers of reaching over a machine with moving parts

    • or being tired or impaired when operating equipment.

    • They must be aware of the possibilities of getting hit by flying chips of metal at any time (and thus the urgent need for safety glasses).

    • They must know how essential it is to clean up spills for the safety of all in the shop.

    • Students must be familiar with the emergency shut off for each machine, and the checklist of items to go over before they even start it.

    • In this context, a brief synopsis of the top four hazards of each machine at start up would be useful for new students. The content expert is seeking an interactive experience, with 360 images and video, as well as knowledge testing built in throughout, and proof that the student has completed the modules on eConestoga. 

Future modules could include: more extensive instruction on the set up and operation of the machines, accident reporting, different machines.

Per Russ, this module needs to be highly MODULAR such that individual topics can be activated and inactivated, depending on the specific program that uses this module. As such, we will rely on the segmented graph concept from Trucking Adv Mode, and keep each segment (each safety topic) completely self-contained so that it can be included or excluded from the module as needed on a per-deployment basis.

Machine Orientation Modules

Note that each of these modules is basically identical in the format and learning objectives

The plan is to design and implement the first of these modules with ALL 3 machines in mind. The second two modules will be copied from the first, and then the content can be customized and the quiz/game can be added. This will ensure a consistent look and feel and will minimize unintended variations between the modules.

The Knowledge Check at the end of each machine safety orientation will be specifically focused on SAFETY knowledge, rather than focusing on procedural knowledge that will be covered in-class.

In all modules, our focus is on the safety precautions and considerations, and will never focus on how to operate any of these machines.

Lathe Safety Orientation

  • Brief orientation to the lathe: What is it used for, what are the major parts, what are specific hazards to be aware of.

  • Locate the emergency shut off for the lathe, identify how and when to use it

  • Understand what hazards to be aware of during operation of the lathe.

  • Photos of the lathe

  • Breakdown of whatever the student needs to know about the machine, what are its major parts, what is it used for. May include video or procedural steps.

  • Photos and specific instructions to activate the emergency shut off.

  • Safety considerations at each stage/step of startup or operation or shutdown as described in the knowledge check activity. The framework for this activity can be the startup or operational SOP, and we can follow that sequence of events to discuss hazards, but we are NOT providing procedural instructions for how to operate the machine at any point.

  1. Click on the photo to identify the emergency shut off.

  2. Select from 4-6 photos to identify a specific hazard of using this machine (e.g. entanglement hazard from spinning work piece - always wear short or fitted sleeves, avoid loose hair, dangling strings, etc.)

  3. Get the lathe ready for use (click on each problem to fix it):

    1. key in chuck

    2. power cord locked out or damaged

    3. debris on the work area

    4. some other problem

Milling Machine Safety Orientation

  • Brief orientation to the milling machine: What is it used for, what are the major parts, what are specific hazards to be aware of.

  • Locate the emergency shut off for the milling machine, identify how and when to use it

  • Understand what hazards to be aware of during operation of the milling machine.

  • Photos of the milling machine

  • Breakdown of whatever the student needs to know about the machine, what are its major parts, what is it used for. May include video or procedural steps.

  • Photos and specific instructions to activate the emergency shut off.

  • Safety considerations at each stage/step of startup or operation or shutdown as described in the knowledge check activity. The framework for this activity can be the startup or operational SOP, and we can follow that sequence of events to discuss hazards, but we are NOT providing procedural instructions for how to operate the machine at any point.

  1. Identify the types of work that a milling machine is used for (3-4 photos of different pieces, 1 is clearly from a milling machine)

  2. Click on the emergency shut off for the milling machine photo

  3. Select from a goup of 4-6 photos to identify a specific hazard of using this machine (e.g. eye injury from flying particles - always wear eye protection in the shop)

Surface Grinder Safety Orientation

  • Brief orientation to the surface grinder: What is it used for, what are the major parts, what are specific hazards to be aware of.

  • Locate the emergency shut off for the surface grinder, identify how and when to use it

  • Understand what hazards to be aware of during operation of the surface grinder.

  • Photos and specific instructions to activate the emergency shut off.

  • Safety considerations at each stage/step of startup or operation or shutdown as described in the knowledge check activity. The framework for this activity can be the startup or operational SOP, and we can follow that sequence of events to discuss hazards, but we are NOT providing procedural instructions for how to operate the machine at any point.

  1. Select the safe clothing choices and PPE for operating the surface grinder (4-6 photos of various clothing and PPE, 3 or 4 are correct choices)

  2. Click to identify the emergency shutoff for the surface grinder.

  3. Match the hazard in the picture (shows an injury or bad outcome) with the correct safety practice (select a good way to prevent that injury or bad outcome)

Proof of Completion

  • There is a legal requirement for the college to demonstrate that the students have completed mandatory safety training. THIS OBJECTIVE IS MET USING A PHYSICAL SIGNATURE AND IS NOT IN SCOPE FOR THIS DLS.

  • Our DLS modules must support this objective by reporting individual learner completion status to eConestoga, but we are not required to obtain e-signatures or provide any physical proof of completion documentation.

  • PRM-02 captures some design ideas and concepts that are relevant to this requirement.

  • Completion status in eConestoga can be used for at-a-glance survey of which students have Not started, Not completed, or Completed the DLS module(s)

  • We can consider adding specific “completion criteria” that will not allow a module to be marked as “Completed” unless certain conditions have been met. This can include quiz questions or minigame success requirements at the segment or module level.

Asset Requirements

At this time, the plan for Precision Machining is that we will not use 3D models at all. The plan is to use 360 photos in an immersive skybox, similar to the orientation modules for Bloom. Learning will be supported through the use of photo and embedded video, with few or no 3D assets or animations required.

This DLS will use voiceover and other audio clips to enhance the immersive experience.

Emphasis will be on Interactive Learning Activities (using the Unity tools that the OMAFRA team is using) so that the DLS is strongly focused on interaction and hands-on practice, rather than passive learning.
