Synopsis of User Feedback - 4 Responses Total from 12 UAT volunteers

  • Most respondents (3/4) had trouble with the DLS.

    • One respondent commented, “The lab is not clear what directions each item should be placed, and never acknowledged a completed step.”

  • Most respondents (3/4) indicated that they got stuck and were unsure how to proceed

    • One respondent indicated, “Check mark doesn't go away. It is very difficult to see how the connection should be done or if one of the check mark does not click then I can't do the next step.”

    • Another respondent commented, “I could not open the PRV all the way, it stays at 0.5 bar.”

  • ALL respondents indicated they encountered issues that prevented them from completing the lab(s).

    • One respondent simply reported, “Either there was a problem or I was completely wrong.”

    • Another respondent commented, “I would say right from the begin. Very little instruction given and no help if not able to proceed.”

  • Most respondents (3/4) indicated that they did not feel the simulation helped them to practice their skills or build confidence.

    • “No, I don't feel like this simulation was helpful to me as a student”

    • “The software is not intuitive and draws on too many preconceptions.”

Analysis of Feedback

After discussion with Sean Yo and Lisa Trimble, our conclusion is that we have experienced some kind of fundamental disconnect in what was expected vs what was delivered. It appears that the simulation was designed and implemented as a “teaching tool” where the instructor would walk students through the required steps of the lab on a shared screen of some kind (in-person or virtual classroom). Lisa seems to recall, in spite of her very limited involvement with this DLS project, that the simulation was intended for use by INSTRUCTORS first and foremost, and that students would only work directly with the simulation after participating in an instructor-led DLS run-through.

In the project Confluence page, there are instructions and diagrams that are referenced as being part of an instructional slide deck that describes the two labs step-by-step. The slide deck does not go into detail about HOW to achieve each step, but it might be assumed that some of that basic knowledge of the components and how they are used would have been taught prior to the introduction of building circuits.

The conclusion, based on the information currently at hand, is that the DLS might not be intended to stand alone as a learning experience, but may only satisfy its learning objectives when accompanied by other learning resources (e.g. a lab booklet that clearly explains how to build each circuit). Alternatively, it may be that this DLS was intended to be used primarily as a teaching aid, with students gaining access to practice independently only AFTER they have completed in-class learning and stepwise introduction to the objectives and procedures for the labs. Another possibility is that we need a pretty serious renovation of the Tutorial, as well as upgrading to the level of guidance provided in the task descriptions and prompts, to help students find their way through this simulation.

Plan of Action

The main objectives for Post-UAT Dev are:

  1. Strip out the task-based graph scenario - Only the Tutorial and Explore Mode will be left. This will eliminate use of the graph scenario and task management system to walk the learner through the two labs - students will be able to rehearse or review ANY lab, using their lab booklet and the virtual hydraulics lab. We will rename Explore Mode to Virtual Lab.

  2. Uplift the Tutorial - the focus of the Tutorial needs to change from teaching about hydraulics components to teaching about how to use the virtual hydraulics lab. This will include a brief review of the available components and their purpose (including a number of small bug fixes for some components and better lighting to visualize all components). However, the main focus of the Tutorial will be a show-then-do type of task-driven tutorial where the student will be taken step by step through all of the basic actions that they will need to perform in the virtual lab in order to build a circuit. This will include steps such as

    1. placing and moving components on the board

    2. making connections

    3. magnifying gauges to take readings

    4. adjusting valves or directional controls on components

    5. disconnecting/removing components from the board

Lisa Trimble and Russ Foubert approved this plan Nov 24, 2021.

Dr. Mirza Ahmed (stakeholder) approved this plan Nov 26, 2021
