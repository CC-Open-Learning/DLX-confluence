VARLab : Release IV - Advanced Mode Concept and Design

Created by Stacey Dineen (Unlicensed), last modified on Mar 13, 2023

In Advanced Mode, our goal is to have the learner inspect the truck and trailer in a free-roaming and self-guided way. This graphic will help compare and contrast the two different play modes, and the reason for each:

In Guided mode, our learner is locked into our linear graph scenario, and they must complete each step of the inspection in a pre-determined order. They cannot choose what to inspect, or where to start. There is no chance to make a mistake or forget anything, because we are guiding the student through the task every step of the way.

In contrast, Advanced mode allows the student to choose their own path. There is no “correct” starting point or ending point for the inspection, the student is free to choose where they want to begin and end.

Problems that we will need to overcome to bring Advanced Mode to life:

  • Graph - Our current graph scenario requires tasks to be completed in order. A new graph scenario would need to be segmented into “positions” where a student can choose to execute certain tasks. For example, while the camera is located at the rear cargo van doors, the student can choose to inspect the rear trailer lights, doors, conspicuity tape, etc. The student is not required to inspect these (no prompts, either text or voiceover will be given) but they can choose to.

  • Task Dependencies - IMPOSSIBLE Some components are only POSSIBLE to inspect if certain conditions are met. For example, it is not possible to inspect the cargo area of the trailer without opening the van doors, because the cargo area is not visible or clickable while the doors are closed. Or, from a procedural standpoint, it is not POSSIBLE to start the truck engine if the brake pedal is not pressed and the gear shift is not in neutral. We need to figure out whether or not students should be allowed to make this type of mistake, and if so, when and how we should give them feedback about their mistake. Like “You forgot to inspect the cargo area because you did not open the cargo van doors”

  • Task Dependencies - UNWISE Other components are POSSIBLE to inspect if certain conditions are not met, but it would be really unwise. For example, the student CAN inspect the truck exterior without first checking to make sure that the parking brakes are applied, but that would be insanely dangerous. It is POSSIBLE to inspect the fan and fan belt while the truck engine is running, but you can’t see very much and you might lose a finger. We need to figure out whether or not students should be allowed to make this type of mistake, and if so, when and how we should give them feedback about their mistake. Like “You died because the trailer brakes were not applied, and you were crushed when the trailer moved unexpectedly while you were inspecting the brakes.

  • Physical Inspections - These are the buttons that trigger animations or inspection activities such as opening a panel, activating lights, defroster, or turning the steering wheel. There are a couple of problems with these: If the camera isn’t looking at the item in question, clicking the physical inspection button will appear to do nothing. The student can’t watch animations that are happening off screen, but they might not remember where to look. Also, there are a lot of physical inspections for the whole truck. On one hand, we don’t want this panel of buttons to be overwhelming and ginormous. On the other hand, we don’t want the buttons to act like a prompt, telling the student what to inspect as they arrive at each position. Tricky.

  • Finish Line - What does DONE look like? We are going to need a way for the student to tell us when they’re done and ready for feedback about their inspection results.

  • Feedback - There will be no instantaneous feedback in the inspection panel dialogue, so we need a way to show the student their results. Maybe a main toolbar button, so they could see their results on demand, but then how do we know when they are done? We might need to save results for only when they say they are finished. Pros and cons both ways - discuss with CE’s.

Advanced Mode Dolly Camera Scheme

The main idea with the Dolly Cam is that it would mimic walking around the entire truck at standing height. The learner can select POI’s around the truck (e.g. DS Engine, Cab Exterior, Rear Trailer, Cab Interior) to act like shortcuts to different areas of the equipment.

At all times, we want the user to be focused on WHAT they want to do, not tied up fiddling around trying to do it. In real life, it is not at all challenging to walk around the truck and see whatever they want to see, so it doesn’t make sense for this to be complicated in the DLX. To serve the learner’s needs, we will have to use a hybrid approach where the user decides what they want to inspect, in what order, and we automate as much of the camera work as we reasonably can to facilitate that.

Default View for Each POI

Using a dropdown menu from the top right hand side of the UI, the learner will be invited to select any location where they want to start. These POI’s do not need to be a 1:1 match for the Inspection Checklist, but they should be arranged in that same general order (this matches Guided Inspection).

*Note that this list is not final, and that locations that are visited more than once (e.g. Cab Interior) can probably just be one POI for Advanced Inspection. The main idea is that the learner can have a list of shortcuts to move around the truck to different physical locations where they can stand and perform inspections in that area.

Each POI in the menu will change appearance slightly (e.g. button background or text colour change) when the learner enters that POI for the first time. This is intended to help the student know where they have been, and if there are any areas of the truck that they have not visited.

This is not a replacement for the Progress Checklist, but it complements that feature by reflecting the tab sequence and helping the user easily see at a glance if there are areas of the truck they have not inspected.

Fine Tuning the View

Within a given POI (or set of POI’s) the learner will be able to use the Move arrows on the screen to move the camera left or right along the dolly track for that POI. The learner will also be able to Zoom (slider) or Pan (click and drag) to adjust their view. These camera controls will be covered in the Advanced Tutorial, but they are really just for finer adjustment of the view. For each POI, the default view will be selected to optimize a good general overview of that area - not zoomed in or focused on any specific inspection. Customized views using Zoom and Pan are automatically reset when moving to a new POI, otherwise we would get some potentially very messed up default views.

Non-Standard Views

Many inspections will require a non-standard view, which is to say that you cannot see what you need to see in the default view for that POI. Inspections that require the camera to be lowered down toward the ground, such as license plates or wheels, as well as inspection points that require an alternate camera location entirely such as brakes, suspension, seatbelt, etc.… will all require a button to activate that specialized view. Similar to a physical inspection, so these buttons will go in the physical inspection tray. For example, when a POI includes one or more wheels, there will be a set of physical inspection buttons available: Tread, Lug Nuts, Inflation, Brakes, Suspension. Clicking on any of these physical inspection buttons will take the learner to a default view (e.g. tire sidewall, rim, lug nuts view, or tread view) and will also trigger the associated animation, if any.

The idea is that the learner is choosing WHAT to inspect and in WHAT ORDER, but we are still facilitating the camera movement to the general area of what they need to see when they choose that inspection. The student is still expected to fine-tune their view, using Zoom, Pan, or even the Move controls if they need a different angle.

Progress Checklist

In Advanced Inspection, students need a way to keep track of what they’ve done so far and what they have left to do. The inspection is long enough that students might often exit partway through and finish another day. The Progress Checklist needs to perform these major functions:

  • List every inspection in the sim, so that the learner can remind themselves of the inspections that are required.

  • Show if the inspections are completed or not, so that the student can quickly see if they have forgotten to inspect anything.

The inspectables are broken down into categories according to where they are located on the truck. Each “tab” of the checklist contains all of the inspections in that POI. NOTE that these tabs are not required to be a 1:1 match for the POI menu in the Adv mode UI, but that would be a nice bonus.

Tabs turn yellow to indicate that all items in that tab have been inspected.

This tab shows that all of the inspectables at the POI “DS Front 2” have been completed (yellow dots)

Note: This is slightly different behaviour from the POI Menu, which updates to indicate that the learner has visited that location. The Progress Checklist is more specific, because it is checking if inspections are completed rather than just tracking the learner’s movement around the truck.

How do we know if an inspection has been completed??

Big question. The fact is that we cannot possibly know for sure if the learner actually inspected anything that they see on their screen. We don’t even know if they are looking at their screen. As a best guess, we will apply the following criteria to update the Progress Checklist during gameplay:

For visual inspections (no physical inspection button to click):

  1. As soon as the learner enters a given POI, all of the visual inspection tasks that are enabled in that POI will be marked as completed. Any disabled visual inspection tasks (see Task Dependencies section below) will not be marked as completed until they have been enabled for at least 5 seconds while the learner is in that POI.

  2. OR if the learner records an inspection result for any inspectable, that inspectable will be marked as completed.

For physical inspections (requires a physical inspection button in the left vertical tray):

  1. As soon as the learner clicks a physical inspection button, that inspectable will be marked as completed. Note that disabled physical inspection buttons cannot be clicked, and so will not be marked as completed unless they are enabled when they are clicked.

  2. If the learner records an inspection result for any inspectable, that inspectable will be marked as completed.

For nested physical inspections (requires initial click on a physical inspection button, which will then bring up a subset of physical inspection buttons inside the specialized view, e.g. Wheels):

  1. As soon as the learner clicks on the first physical inspection button (e.g. Wheels) the inspections that are visible in that specialized view (e.g. sidewall and rim) will be marked as completed.

  2. Nested physical inspections inside the specialized view must be clicked in order for those inspectables to be marked as completed, OR an inspection result is recorded for a nested physical inspection.

Task Dependencies

In Guided Mode, the learner is guided through all of the required inspections IN ORDER. This means that the learner is not responsible to decide WHEN to inspect anything, or in what order. In the Advanced Inspection, the learner will be allowed to decide for themselves how they want to inspect the truck, which means that they may forget some inspections entirely or do some inspections incorrectly. This is OK! A big part of learning is making mistakes, and this DLX is a safe and cost-effective way to make those errors.

Some inspections are inefficient or silly if you do them in the wrong order. For example, you can go around the entire truck and trailer and mark every single light as “Burnt out”, even if you are inspecting them without ever turning them on. This is a silly thing to do, but it’s not dangerous or impossible, so we’re going to allow students to make those types of mistakes.

This “Task Dependencies” section is only interested in inspections that are actually NOT POSSIBLE to do while the engine is in the wrong state, or are dangerous enough that we will not allow the student to practice doing them improperly. We aren’t trying to guide the learner to do things in the correct/best order, because that’s what Guided Inspection is for.

This list may be incomplete. If you are aware of a Task Dependency that is not listed here, please contact Stacey Dineen (Unlicensed) or Ujjwal Prashar (Unlicensed) for further discussion.

Cold vs Warm inspections

In Guided Mode, we separate both the engine and the cab interior inspection POI’s into two distinct phases: Cold (engine not running) and Warm (engine running). For most of the inspection tasks at these two POI’s, it isn’t actually crucial that the inspections have to be done in a specific order. However, some inspection tasks will give incorrect results or be very dangerous if they are done at the wrong time. We have to find a way to acknowledge that fact in Advanced Inspection, without overly restricting or guiding the learner.

Required COLD inspections: These inspection tasks will only be enabled when the engine is NOT running. Meaning, the physical inspection button (if any) will not be enabled while the engine is running, and it will not be possible to click on or report any inspection results for these components while the engine is running. If the mouse hovers over the physical inspection button or the inspectable component while the inspection is disabled, a tooltip will be shown with a simple message such as “Engine must be turned off”:

  • Steering wheel - Because power steering is activated when the truck is running, it is important to test the looseness of the steering wheel when the engine is turned off. Power steering makes the steering wheel turn easily at all times, so you would not be able to tell if the wheel was loose.

  • Fan & fan belt - It is not physically possible to do a visual inspection of the fan blades or fan belt while the engine is running - they are moving too fast to see. Also, it is very dangerous to touch or get too close to the fan or fan belt while the engine is running, you could become entangled and lose a finger.

  • Engine oil - Of course, it is POSSIBLE to check the engine oil anytime, but we want students to understand that they must always check the engine oil level prior to starting the engine, to avoid excessive wear and tear on the engine. Students are required to top up the engine oil prior to starting the engine, so we can’t let them wait until after the engine is running to check the level - that’s not logical.

Required WARM inspections: These inspection tasks will only be enabled when the engine is running. Meaning, the physical inspection button (if any) will not be enabled unless the engine is running, and it will only be possible to click on or report any inspection results for these components while the engine is running. If the mouse hovers over the physical inspection button or the inspectable component while the inspection is disabled, a tooltip will be shown with a simple message such as “Engine must be running”:

  • Air leaks or wiring electrical faults - It is not possible to inspect for air leaks or electrical faults while the engine is not running because there would not be air pressure in all the lines or current in all wiring.

  • Air pressure gauge - The compressors need to be running in order to check the air pressure gauges on the dashboard.

  • Oil pressure gauge - The engine oil is not under pressure when the engine is not running, so it is not possible to get a reading on the gauge until after the engine has been started.

  • Defroster temperature - The defroster is a bizarre exception because it just needs time to warm up. It doesn’t matter if the engine is running or not (it might have mattered a long time ago, but these days the defroster is electrical and does not rely on warmth from the engine block), it will always start by blowing cold air and will gradually warm up over about 5 minutes. The easiest way for us to handle this is to simply pretend that this inspection requires the engine to be running, and I would be satisfied with that as a compromise. To really really make this work like it does in real life, we would need a timer to start when the defroster is activated, and it would not be available to check until at least 180-240 seconds had gone by.

  • Brake lines - Just like the air leaks under the hood, the brake lines on all the wheels rely on air pressure from the compressor. If the engine is not running, the pressure is not up, and leaks would not be detectable. In our Adv Inspection, all of the inspections behind the wheel (axle seals, brake chambers, brake drums, and brake lines) are clumped together, so we probably shouldn’t disable the physical inspection button. We just need to prevent anyone from being able to click on or report a problem with the air lines.
