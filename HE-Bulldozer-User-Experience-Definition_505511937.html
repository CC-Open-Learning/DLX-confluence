VARLab : HE-Bulldozer User Experience Definition

Created by Stacey Dineen (Unlicensed), last modified on Oct 24, 2022

Initial View and Welcome

On initial load, the camera will approach the vehicle in a cinematic fashion as though the student is walking towards it from a distance of approx 20-40m. This is from the student's point of view, at approx eye level, lasting no more than a few seconds to help orient the learner to the simulation environment.

The voiceover will welcome the student and advise them to follow the prompts to practice a walk-around inspection of the bulldozer. Students will be prompted to observe each inspectable item in order.

Camera Behaviour and Navigation

The HE-Bulldozer simulation will primarily be a Guided Mode style of learner interaction, where the camera will move automatically for each inspection, there will be no need for the learner to navigate manually using either keyboard or mouse. The Guided Mode inspection walks the student through the inspection process so that the student does not decide what to inspect or in what order. For certain views, we will consider the optional use of camera pan and zoom, but the objective will always be to ensure that the learner can see whatever they need to inspect without fiddling around with the camera.

Interaction Model

TBD - With the inclusion of multi-media visual experiences for the learner, it is unclear if our traditional user interaction for inspections will be appropriate.

Traditionally, we have shown the user a visual representation (a 3D model) of the inspectable component, and asked them to determine if the component was good or bad (and to specify the type of “bad” state shown). This method requires intricately detailed models, sometimes involving several different models of the exact same component to represent the good and bad states. Damages had to sometimes be exaggerated, even to an unrealistic extent, in order to be reasonably noticeable to the learner.

In this new mixed-media simulation, we might consider a totally different approach to teaching the pre-op inspection process, with a more comprehensive view. For example, we could consider the popularity of the Results Panel design that was used for Trucking Pre-Trip Inspection. That panel shows the GOOD state of the selected component as well as ALL OF THE BAD STATES. This helps the learner to identify the key differences between the good and bad states for the selected inspectable component, and can use photos, videos, still shots captured from video footage, or even illustrations or diagrams to get the point across.

Another area for consideration is the learner feedback model for this inspection. Our e-learning experts, Lisa and our other resources in eLC, can help direct us toward the most effective model for providing feedback and redirection for learners.

Progress and Feedback

We may consider reuse of the Progress Checklist and Results Panel that were used in the Trucking Pre-Trip Inspection simulation:

Progress Checklist - The primary function of the Progress Checklist is to help the student identify what they have already completed in their inspection workflow, and what is left to be done. The Progress Checklist does not indicate if answers are correct or incorrect, and does not need to provide any details about what damages have been reported. The student is free to revisit any part of the inspection at any time, so they can review/change their answers by simply revisiting a POI.

When students complete this daily pre-op inspection in person, they are required to fill out a physical checklist like this sample (shown below). We might find it helpful to design our progress checklist in a similar way, just for familiarity. Notice that the student must enter the equipment # and the current mileage/hours for the equipment. It might be valuable for us to include finding & recording these two pieces of information during our simulated inspection, so that students can practice where to find them.

Results Panel - Sometimes the inspection workflow does not provide any immediate feedback to the learner about their selected answer for any of the inspection tasks, so the Results Panel is the primary way for the learner to see their results. Other times, feedback is provided in real time and the Results Panel is a summary view of all feedback up to this point. On demand (usually a menu button), the student will be shown a complete list of every inspection task (these may be organized in a familiar way that is consistent with the Progress Checklist, to help the student find what they are looking for) where each task will be given one of THREE results:

  • Green checkmark - This task was completed successfully. The selected answer is the correct answer.

  • Red X - This task was completed, but the answer selected was incorrect. The correct answer will be provided at this time.

  • Incomplete - This result will be assigned to any inspection POI that was NOT VISITED by the student. This is an important distinction because we want the student to get a different result when they missed/forgot a step in the inspection, even if there was no damage present at that POI in this case.

Position #1 - Front Fluid Leaks

The camera will approach the dozer model as though the learner is walking toward the equipment from the front “driver” side, meaning the left-hand side of the dozer from the perspective of an operator sitting in the driver’s seat. This first-person view should be at human height, providing a realistic view of what an operator would see as they walk up to the bulldozer when it is parked. The camera will stop at the front “driver” side area of the vehicle, angled so that the camera is looking at the ground behind the bulldozer blade. Example:

From here, the student will be prompted by the voiceover to perform inspections in this immediate area. Note that the student can pan and zoom the camera to look around the area of the default camera position:

  1. Fluid Leaks (note if present) under the dozer body or in this area

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #2 - Rear Fluid Leaks

The camera will pan smoothly along the driver’s side exterior, maintaining a first-person eye-level view of the equipment at all times. The camera will stop at the rear of the dozer, looking at the ground underneath the dozer body at the rear of the equipment.

From here, the student will be prompted by the voiceover to perform inspections in this immediate area. Note that the student can pan and zoom the camera to look around the area of the default camera position:

  1. Fluid Leaks (note if present) under the dozer body in this area

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #3 - Engine Compartment

The camera moves smoothly up the far side of the dozer to the engine compartment access door. The access panel opens and the student is prompted (one at a time) to perform the following inspections:

  1. oil check (low)

  2. Wiring (loose, damaged)

  3. Fuel water separator(s) (water or dirt present in bowl)

  4. Leaks in the engine compartment (some minor spillage around oil filler port is normal, but should not look excessive or fresh)

  5. Fan belt, fan, fan shroud (belt damaged, fan blades damaged, fan shroud damaged)

  6. Hoses and wiring (damaged, loose)

  7. Air filter - squeeze the collection end to release excess dirt

  8. Coolant reservoir - difficult to read the coolant level from this angle, but you can check for leaks or damage to the underside of the reservoir from this vantage point.

  9. Check coolant (this may require access through a different panel) by opening filler cap to see fluid level and make sure there is no debris/contaminant in the fluid (low, debris in reservoir) . Note this can only be done when the machine is cold, never open the coolant reservoir when hot.

  10. At this point, the panel swings closed and the learner is prompted to inspect the access panel door, hinges, latch (abraded, dented, damaged, missing fasteners, latch function)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #4 - Hydraulics Compartment

The camera moves smoothly back toward the rear of the equipment, to the side compartment to the rear of the cab door on the “passenger” side of the dozer. The access panel opens and the student is prompted (one at a time) to inspect the following components:

  1. Fuel water separator (dirt or water in the bowl)

  2. Hydraulic fluid sight glass (low or high) Note that ANYTHING between high and low is “good” - it is not necessary for the fluid level to be exactly in the middle where it says “FULL”.

  3. Hydraulic hoses and connections (leaking, damaged)

  4. At this point, the panel swings closed and the learner is prompted to inspect the access panel door, hinges, latch, and surrounding body panels (abraded, dented, damaged, missing fasteners, latch function)

  5. Hydraulic fluid filler port (clean, good condition)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #5 - Fuel Filler Port

The camera moves smoothly around to the rear of the dozer to focus on the fuel filler port. The learner is prompted to inspect the component:

  1. Fuel filler port (cap is missing, cap is loose)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #6 - Battery Compartment

The camera moves smoothly to the “driver’s” side battery compartment, and the compartment door swings open. The learner is prompted to inspect (one at a time) the components in this immediate area:

  1. Master switch - check to make sure it is ON

  2. Transmission fluid sight glass, if present (low or high) - Note that ANYTHING between high and low is “good” - it is not necessary for the fluid level to be exactly in the middle where it says “FULL”. Also, not all machines have separate transmission and hydraulics sight glass. CAT does not have two separate sight glass gauges, because it uses the same fluid for everything.

  3. Wiring (loose, frayed)

  4. Battery connections (loose) - Unscrew cover and lift, then grab onto battery cables and wiggle them back and forth to ensure they are securely attached to the battery terminals. Reattach as needed before replacing cover and securing in place with clips or screws

  5. At this point, the panel swings closed and the learner is prompted to inspect the access panel door, hinges, latch, and surrounding body panels (abraded, dented, damaged, missing fasteners, latch function)

  6. Transmission filler port, if present - not all machines have this (clean, not damaged)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #7 - Cab Interior 1

The camera moves smoothly to the front driver’s side of the dozer, behind the blade, where the learner is prompted to inspect the equipment for safe entry:

  1. Operator steps and handholds for climbing into the cab (bent or broken, unsafe stepping surface, loose or damaged handholds)

After completing that inspection, the student is prompted to climb up to the cab door. The voiceover reminds the learner to always maintain 3 points of secure contact when climbing into or out of the dozer. The camera follows the first-person view of climbing onto the dozer to the driver’s side door. From this vantage point, the learner will be prompted to complete the following inspections:

  1. Cab door (latch, hinges, frame)

  2. Operator’s seat is intact and secure. Seatbelt is present and functional (??? Is this a thing? What else do we need to check??)

  3. After inspecting the seat/seat belt, the camera moves smoothly into a first-person view from sitting in the operator’s seat. The learner is prompted to turn the key to start the engine. Note the gauges sweep and the warning lights are all shown again briefly. Once the panel is initialized, the student can take note of the oil pressure, fuel reading, temperature reading, and check for any warning lights that remain lit. Parking brake light stays ON until the parking brake is released. Seat belt light stays ON until the operator seat belt is engaged.

  4. Learner is prompted to activate the exterior lights and to leave the engine running so that it can warm up. While the engine is warming up, the learner is prompted to check the transmission temperature gauge (example shown below)

  5. Learner is prompted to check the throttle to ensure it is set to zero.

  6. Check drive controls are securely attached and moving as expected

  7. Check reverse alarm and horn

  8. Hydraulic controls are securely attached and moving as expected

  9. Windshield wiper(s) make sure they are present and functional. If the dozer has windshield washer feature, it should be tested now as well. (fluid empty)

  10. At this point, the learner is prompted to exit the cab to continue the circle check, including a reminder to maintain 3 secure points of contact at all times.

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #8 - Exterior body condition DSF

The camera follows smoothly as the user exits the cab of the dozer to return to a standing position beside the front driver’s side of the dozer. This vantage point will be approximately beside the dozer blade (the learner has just stepped down from the step behind the dozer blade) looking at an angle. Similar angle to this screenshot, but the camera will be closer to the equipment.

From this vantage point, the learner is prompted to inspect:

  1. Exterior body panels (dented, abraded, severely corroded, missing bolts)

  2. Windows (obstructed by mud, cracked)

  3. Exterior lights (burned out)

  4. Cylinders - pistons, hoses and welds (bent, scored, leaking, weld lines broken)

  5. Guards (bent, missing bolts, damaged) and warning labels (unreadable)

  6. Pins and bushings (pins missing, bushings excessively worn)

  7. ROPS and roof from DSF angle (no severe damage - rust and small dings are NORMAL)

Note that the wipers are only inspected from inside the cab, when they can be turned on. There is no need to inspect the wipers as part of the exterior check. When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #9 - Undercarriage DS

The camera moves smoothly to focus on the undercarriage on the “driver’s side” of the equipment, at the location where the learner is standing after exiting the cab. The learner is prompted (one at a time) to inspect the following items at this location:

  1. Final drive sprocket (pointy teeth are excessively worn, loose bolts showing shiny metal)

  2. Track pins and bushings (bushings excessively worn, pins missing)

  3. Rollers (incorrectly aligned so that they are not in good contact with with the track, leaking)

  4. Track pads (uneven wear, appear crooked, damaged, loose)

  5. Track bolts (loose so that shiny metal is showing or does not line up properly, missing)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #10 - Front Grill and Coolers

The camera moves smoothly to the front of the bulldozer body, where the learner is prompted to inspect the following items:

  1. Grill (damaged, dented, obstructed with excessive debris)

  2. Coolers - learner must look THROUGH the grill to see the coolers. (leaking, damaged/missing fins, obstructed by excessive debris)

Note that it is the coolers that may leak and create a puddle on the ground that is inspected in Position #1 of this inspection process. We don’t inspect the coolers until after the engine has been running for a few minutes, because coolant might not leak while the engine is not running. Leaks might be visible only on the cooler exterior, without forming a puddle on the ground, which is why we check for both.

Position #11 - Bulldozer Blade

The camera moves smoothly to the front of the bulldozer blade.

The student is prompted to inspect the following items at this location:

  1. Blade surface (severe dents or breakage of the blade surface, NOTE that minor dents are NORMAL, do not need to be reported)

  2. Bolts (missing or loose, sticking out)

  3. Blade edge (excessively worn, uneven)

  4. Rear blade (pins missing, severe dents or breakage)

  5. Cylinders - pistons, hoses and welds (bent, scored, leaking, weld lines broken)

  6. Guards (bent, missing bolts, damaged) and warning labels (unreadable)

  7. Pins and bushings (pins missing, bushings excessively worn)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #12 - Exterior body condition PSF

The camera follows smoothly as the user exits the cab of the dozer to return to a standing position beside the front driver’s side of the dozer. From this vantage point, the learner is prompted to inspect:

  1. Exterior body panels (dented, abraded, severely corroded, missing bolts)

  2. Windows (obstructed by mud, cracked)

  3. Exterior lights (burned out)

  4. Cylinders - pistons, hoses and welds (bent, scored, leaking, weld lines broken)

  5. Guards (bent, missing bolts, damaged) and warning labels (unreadable)

  6. Pins and bushings (pins missing, bushings excessively worn)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #13 - Undercarriage PS

The camera moves smoothly to focus on the undercarriage on the “passenger side” of the equipment, where the same inspections that were done on the DS are repeated.

  1. Final drive sprocket (pointy teeth are excessively worn, loose bolts showing shiny metal)

  2. Track pins and bushings (bushings excessively worn, pins missing)

  3. Rollers (incorrectly aligned so that they are not in good contact with with the track, leaking)

  4. Track pads (uneven wear, appear crooked, damaged, loose)

  5. Track bolts (loose so that shiny metal is showing or does not line up properly, missing)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #14 - Exterior body condition PSR

The camera moves smoothly to the rear PS corner of the dozer. From this vantage point, the learner is prompted to inspect:

  1. Exterior body panels (dented, abraded, severely corroded, missing bolts)

  2. Windows (obstructed by mud, cracked)

  3. Exterior lights (burned out)

  4. Counterweight, if present (secure)

  5. Guards (bent, missing bolts, damaged) and warning labels (unreadable)

  6. ROPS and roof from PSR angle (no severe damage - rust and small dings are NORMAL)

When these inspections are completed, the student will be prompted to move on to the next position when they are ready to continue.

Position #15 - Track Tension including cinematic prep for this inspection

Cinematic (this can play without any intervention from our learner, it is intended only to represent to correct method of preparing for this inspection): The camera moves smoothly back to the DS cab entry door and follows as the learner moves to a first-person view as sitting in the operator seat. The learner is prompted to follow the procedure described in the operator’s manual to move the dozer forward, backward, or both, to facilitate inspection of the track tension. The parking brake light on the dash display goes out, signifying that the parking brake is being released. Some kind of animation or video will represent the learner moving the dozer (remember to use the reverse alarm if the dozer reverses at any point) . After movement is completed, the parking brake light on the dash will come back on - signifying that the parking brake is being reapplied. The camera will exit the cab of the dozer (voiceover will remind the learner to maintain 3 points of secure contact when entering or exiting the cab). The camera will stop beside the dozer at standing height as the learner is standing at the DS track.

  1. DS track tension (too loose, too tight) - follow operator’s manual to assess the proper tension

  2. Camera moves smoothly around the rear of the dozer to the PS track for the same inspection (too loose, too tight).

When these inspections are completed, the student will be congratulated on completing the bulldozer daily pre-op inspection. Results will be shown.
