VARLab : Traffic 2.0: Field Research with Students

Created by Sana Javeed, last modified on Jun 18, 2024

Date: Feb 2, 2024

This report contains details of the field research conducted with students in-class for testing the Traffic 2.0 DLX with 60 students for 1 hour over 2 days with 28 students on day1 and 32 students on day 2.

A survey was also shared providing valuable insights into their experiences and perceptions of the simulation. This report aims to summarize and analyse the feedback, highlighting key areas of improvement and positive aspects.

Research Goals & Questions

Research Goals

Research Questions

Ease of use & Navigation (User Experience)

Find out how do students navigate using the DLX in order to complete tasks. How successful are they and how do they go about completing an investigation?

Can the students navigate the scenes independently (i.e. does the tutorial provide enough info)?

With what ease or difficulty are the students able to complete the investigation tasks? What issues do they run into (if any) when completing tasks?

Promotes learning (Instructional Design)

 Discover the aspects of design that support their learning achieved from the DLX

Are the students able to understand what is required to achieve the learning outcomes defined?

Does the email provide them with what they need to complete their assignment (the traffic report)?

Engagement Factor (Game Design)

 Understand how users stay engaged to complete an investigation while also determining the pain points encountered.

Do they understand the controls that are being used in the DLX? Do they have any pain points?

Do they employ any workarounds in the DLX?

User Insights

Executive Summary 

Almost all (41) participants responded to the survey and 8 students who participated in the class were observed through the notes. Hence the data below is based on 49 students for qualitative and 41 based on quant. Around 90% played the Normal mode. Users appreciated the simulation for its improved pacing, user-friendly tutorials, and impressive graphics, with participants expressing satisfaction. However, high-priority issues were identified through both qualitative and quantitative data.

There were 15 issues in total, out of which 5 are high priority, 7 low priority and 3 low priority issues. Some of the listed issues were.

  • Prolonged loading times affected 8 students, with 3 unable to play.

  • Glitches and lagging responses were reported by 18 students, including frozen screens and disappearing cursors.

  • Significant delays in email delivery affected 3 students, while 14 received no emails upon completion.

  • Mac users encountered challenges with right-click functionality, and navigation.

  • Overall :

    No. of students who completed atleast one investigation – 27 

    No. of students who failed to complete the investigations – 7

    No. of students who failed to receive emails - 14 

Among the 24 students (N= 41) who found it neutral to difficult, there were complaints about difficulties in navigation and following instructions. Many expressed uncertainties about what to do next, finding the process somewhat confusing and lacking clarity.

Out of the 25 students (N=41) who found it easy to very easy, they appreciated the clear and intuitive instructions, easy-to-follow subtitles, and well-explained hints. However, the remaining students encountered delays and technical challenges, particularly with the controls. Issues included difficulty in looking around with the camera and using the right-click function to stop, especially on laptops. Additionally, many students experienced significant lagging during the simulation.

First Impressions

The initial impressions of the simulation varied among participants. Some found it well-planned, interesting, and fun, while others experienced issues such as glitches, lag, and confusion with instructions. Overall, opinions ranged from positive to negative, with a mix of praise for its concept and criticism for its technical execution.

Positive Aspects

Successful completion & Satisfaction: 

27 students out of 41 students (66%) successfully completed all steps and expressed satisfaction with the simulation. 

“It’s cool to be able to investigate a collision” 

“Real-life scenarios, problem solving ideas and proper protocols that are put in place. These all will help in my future career” 

Improved Pace: 

Positive feedback received regarding the improved pace compared to previous simulations. 

Students appreciated the pacing, describing it as "great." 

“It was pretty good compared to others I've used” 

User-Friendly Tutorial: 

Students found the tutorial easy to understand and straightforward. 

Clear instructions during the tutorial contributed to a positive learning experience. 

“Everything. The set up was easy to use, and I didn't have any troubles in the simulation.” 


“I like that there was a tutorial teaching you as the simulation is happening and that there were practice simulations to do” 

Graphics Quality: 

Positive feedback on the graphics quality of the simulation. 

Appreciation for visually appealing elements enhancing the overall experience. 

“Cool Graphics and free range to do investigation.” 

“I liked the look and feel of the simulation.” 

Issues

  1. High Priority Issue: Critical usability problems that significantly impact user experience and must be addressed urgently.

  2. Medium Priority Issue: Usability issues that affect user experience but may not be as critical as high priority issues and can be addressed in the near future.

  3. Low Priority Issue: Minor usability issues that have minimal impact on user experience and can be addressed at a later stage when resources permit.

High Priority Issues

User Quotes

DLX Load Time Delay: 8 students complained about the delay while loading the simulation 

(3) Some students couldn't play due to persistent loading issues. 

“I was not able to attempt it” 

Frozen Simulation and Glitches: 18 Instances of the simulation freezing, requiring restarts, disappearing mouse cursors.

Reports of lag and glitches affecting the ability to walk and look simultaneously. 

Students were unable to enter login details as the screen froze. 

“Glitchy, not being able to move, not being able to look around” 

“Hard to load, very laggy, only works on certain devices.” 

Email Delivery Issues/failure: 3 users stated a Prolonged delays in receiving emails, while 14 users (N=41) claimed to have not received any email at all.

"In a previous simulation, I received an email instantly" 

Using previously known controls that close the current tab: 

Students attempted to use controls from a different/last simulation, leading to confusion and disruptions.  eg: using the crouch feature (ctrl-S), which just kicked him out of the sim. 

Control used CTRL+W to crouch and move forward to get a better view of the tires in car collision resulted in closing of the tab and logs them out of the DLX again. 

Error message: An error screen pops out at the end of the sims (e.g., &&^%#675476777874). Seems to repeat a couple of times.

Medium Priority Issues

User Quotes

Forced Logout Issues:  100% students faced forced logouts, disrupting their workflow. Hence forcing them to log in each time.

MAC User Issues:

Difficulty in right-clicking (two fingers) for Mac users, Felt frustrated (14 students). 

[Design Recommendation: Supplemental information required by instructor to students] 

“Yes, I did on MacBook and it was very difficult to do this investigation” 

“a bit frustrating at first to navigate on a Mac but as I went along it was good” 

Annoyed by right-click each time to navigate

“It was a little tricky with the camera, it’s quite annoying to right click every time I want to move” 

Full Screen expand: Students didn’t know how to go full screen, approximately 10 were observed struggling with it. 

  • Students couldn't go Fullscreen when pressing the right button. 

“Just the bug where it wouldn't open in full screen” 

Cognitive overload on instructions:

Couple of students found instructions to be too detailed. 

[Design Recommendation: Provide short just in time instructions to reduce cognitive overload] 

Instructions not in-sync to the action:

10 students encountered difficulties following the tutorial steps, where the next instruction appeared before completing the current step.

eg: A student struggled as the on-screen instruction directed to click the briefing button without completing the previous step (right-click to get back to the tab). Another student faced a similar issue during the measurement tool step of the tutorial.

Uncertainty in Starting and Progressing: 

While all students played the tutorial, 5 students reported challenges in understanding the order of tasks and uncertainty upon completing a scene. 

Some attributed the confusion to the necessity of prior subject matter knowledge before engaging with the DLX.

“I was unsure if I was finished with the scene or not” 

“There were not many challenges, however completing the investigation in order was difficult.” 

Low Priority Issues

User Quotes

13.

Unexpected Behavior for Measurements task: 2 Students faced challenges with unexpected behavior like using the 'X' button instead of point-and-click.

[Design Recommendation: Having an accessible way like providing a delete button on hover on the measurement ] 

“Only cancelling the measuring thing after I was done measuring. should have a cancel button on the keyboard for most things” 

Mouse/Camera Sensitivity: Approx 5 students complained about the camera spinning too fast, leading to discomfort.

When they were prompted to change it in the settings, to which the term “look sensitivity” seemed confusing.

[Design Recommendation: Slowing down the pace a little notch down and changing the term “look sensitivity” to “ camera speed” ] 

“I was confused first time seeing. My cursor was everywhere” 

“The pace of the camerais too fast”

Slow speed of tutorials and Interviews: It was reported that some students would have preferred the pace of the tutorial to be a little quicker. 

“Quicker to read than listen to the audios in the interview”

Lessons Learnt

  1. It was helpful to re-evaluate the time allotted to the students for testing from day 1 to day 2 of testing. The time was shortened by 30 mins, hence 1 hour was sufficient - time included pre-brief+ de-brief+ student testing +survey responses.

  2. In this situation, a class testing seemed to be the feasible option. Various factors depending could be the stage project scope and budget. However, prototype testing and regular testing at an early stage could be fruitful.

  3. Use of sticky notes for student numbering facilitated anonymity and captured both quantitative and qualitative insights effectively.

  4. Testing with students helped confirm and validate the observations made previously by the team.

  5. Testing should be a part of the process at every stage - to catch on any early problems that can be retrofitted within project scope and budget. Eg. Discovered great insights like CTRl+W causing the tab to close causing a hindrance which should have been detected earlier.

FUTURE OPPORTUNITIES

  1. It would be helpful to get feedback when used in real time intentions for an assignment. A survey to be shared with the students would also support further insights. Currently, some students didn't think about seriously playing the simulation the minute they knew there were no assignment deliverables required.
