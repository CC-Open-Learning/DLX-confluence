11 Jun 2024

Important design decisions this week:

  • SPECIAL CAMERA POSITIONS: Some inspections that we want to include in the DLX will not work from the default camera position. The most prominent examples are the wheel inspections. Because the default camera position is at standing height, the viewing angle for the wheels is just not going to provide an adequate view for the learner to inspect. So, in a design meeting with Kim, we decided on the following:

    • Anytime an inspection requires a special view to inspect, the user can enter a special close-up camera position simply by clicking on the inspectable itself OR by clicking on a tool icon in a the main toolbar. The specialized view will not happen in a separate window or panel, we will just move the camera into position. To exit from this special camera position, the learner will need to click on a Back or Done button.

    • Inspectables that have a special view will not be “fixable” in the default camera view. It is not likely that a user would be able to clearly see damage without entering the special view, but if they did, they would not be able to “fix” the damage in without entering the special camera position. Clicking on the item in the default view (regardless if it is in good or bad state) will take you into the special view. To “fix” a bad state, the learner would need to click on it in the special camera view, then it will be “fixed”. Toolbar icon is required because users are not likely to click on the wheel at all if it appears to be in good condition. The toolbar icon will act as a reminder that they need to take a closer look at some of the inspectables.

    • Toolbar icons will be action-oriented and item-specific (e.g. Inspect DS Steer Tire, Inspect DS Rear Tractor Tires, etc.) and will have tooltips on hover to help the user discover their purpose. Toolbar icons will dynamically appear or become enabled on the toolbar when the user enters the POI where those inspectables are located. It will not be possible to access the DS Cab inspectables when you are at a different POI. It is possible that some inspectables will be accessible from multiple POI’s but only if they could relalistically be inspected from more than one location in real life.

    • The wheels will only have a limited number of inspections enabled, because we don’t want to have to move the camera again once we are in the special view. The special view for the wheels will be a side-on view, so the user will be able to click to identify the following types of issues:

      • Sidewall (cracked)

      • Rim (dented, cracked)

      • Lug nuts (NO animation - the only bad state for lug nuts is “missing”)

      • We will NOT inspect: tread, inflation, debris, or brakes

  • Feedback - Inspection Report

    • This is a very simple checkmark/X type of feedback that will be used in both modules. The simple point-click module will rely entirely on the inspection report feedback to communicate success to the user, while the immersive narrative module will include a post-inspection cutscene that will show the user the consequences of their work before showing the report feedback.

    • The feedback will just be a complete list of all inspectables, organized by POI. Everthing that is in good condition is automatically a checkmark (correct) regardless if the user clicks on them or not. The inspectables that are in bad condition will only be marked as correct (checkmark) if they were clicked on. If they were not clicked on, then they are incorrect (ex).

31 May 2024

  • First production sprint is a wild success! Playable demo has been posted to VARLAB-DEV1.

  • Important design decision was made this week: In the previous Trucking inspection, it was really important for students to be able to describe the nature of the problem, so they would have to select from various damaged states such as dented vs abraded, burnt out vs damaged, etc. We reviewed this learning goal and recognized that this is not the same for our CCSIF research user experience. In this DLX, the primary goal for our user is to FIND bad/worn/damaged inspectable components, but we aren’t really worried about them being able to classify or provide details about the type of damage or problem that is present. Instead of having them inspect everything and declare it to be good or bad, we will only worry about having them find the bad stuff.

  • In order to facilitate the immersive storytelling outcomes for the second DLX module, we need to make sure that the truck is in good repair at the end of the inspection. For this reason, when the user clicks on something that is bad/damaged in our DLX, it will be magically fixed (bad state will be replaced with good state). Many bad states are decals, so those are easy to hide. In other cases, we might actually need to swap the model, but both methods would have the same outcome. When you click on something damaged, it gets magically fixed! If you find all of the bad/damaged states, the truck will be in perfect condition at the end of the inspection and you can drive off to the “happy trails” ending described below.

  • We can pair this with a success sfx and/or onscreen toast message that says “Damaged <thing> has been repaired!”

  • If the learner clicks on too many undamaged “good” inspectable components, we can prompt them with a message like “<This thing> is in good condition! Find items that are damaged or worn out!” or something along those lines, just to keep them on track and not clicking everything blindly.

Type of Solution

Procedural - Flatscreen

(PoV) (3D Models & Assets)

This project will be a virtual exterior inspection of a class 8 transport truck, with the learner acting in the role of operator/inspector.

Similar to the Trucking Pre-Trip Inspection simulation, this experience will be dramatically simplified and truncated so that it can be completed in approx 20-30 minutes, even by a novice user. The same experience will then be rebuilt as a separate standalone DLX, this time including narrative storytelling and gamification elements that are designed to engage the learner on a cognitive as well as affective level. These two modules will comprise the DLX.

This list will describe some of the basic elements for the first module, but the actual requirements will be documented in the SharePoint workspace:

  1. Flat screen DLX - will be used online by remote users. Unlike many production DLX projects, this DLX will not be used by Conestoga College students, and will not be deployed in eConestoga. This research DLX will be published to WebGL and SCORM-wrapped just like other DLX, but then it will be deployed via Moodle.

  2. 360* immersive environment with point-click navigation. Using only a mouse, the learner is able to walk around the virtual learning space, and pan/zoom the camera to observe their environment. No game controllers will be used, only basic keyboard and mouse interactions will be required.

  3. Art: We will use the commercial transport truck with attached cargo van trailer from the Trucking Pre-Trip Inspection simulation. Only the exterior and wheels models are required - we will not be going into the cab interior, opening the cargo van doors, underneath the truck or trailer, or under the hood. This will save us a lot of time making new 3D assets. All accompanying damaged states, including decals and textures can also be borrowed from that project. No new inspections or bad states will be required for this research DLX.

  4. No bad state sets are required for this DLX. Each player is expected to play only one module, although they can play it as many times as they want. There is no need to change the good/bad states for any inspectable, they can be the same every time. The goal of this project is NOT to provide a full inspection experience, but to compare procedural learning outcomes between two different types of DLX. It is expected that each POI should have no more than 4-8 inspection tasks, and there should be no more than about 6 POI’s.

  5. Learner can navigate freely to different POIs around the truck using point-click navigation, e.g. Front Driver’s side Cab, Rear Passenger Side Trailer, and be offered inspection tasks to complete at each POI. The order of POI’s does not matter, and the learner is allowed to skip around and return to previously visited POIs without limitations.

  6. We may wish to consider a very basic checklist-style progress indicator that could help the learner identify if they have completed all inspection tasks at each POI.

  7. It seems unlikely that our learner would benefit from Save & Load, even if this feature could be made to work outside of eConestoga (it is likely this experience will be deployed in Moodle). This might be nice to have, but is definitely not a hard requirement.

  8. When the learner is ready, they will end the inspection and receive feedback. The feedback should be very basic, just correct/incorrect/not inspected for each inspection task at each POI.

  9. Analytics must record the total # of unique learners to access the DLX and the total # of instructional minutes they spent in the DLX. This may be achieved using a play timer or diagetic clock in the simulation, or it might be achievable using PlayFab features.

Objectives for CCSIF Trucking Research Project

The objective for this research project will be to compare the user experience and learning outcomes for two digital learning experiences, where the learning objectives and content of each module are identical, but the user is engaged differently in each module.

MODULE 1: Simple DLX - In this virtual inspection, the learner will be provided with only necessary information or prompts, but there will be no narrative element to connect the tasks, or to provide motivation or situational outcomes. Very basic feedback (correct/incorrect/not completed) will be provided at the end of the inspection to identify inspection tasks that were completed correctly or incorrectly, or tasks that were missed. Emotional or situational engagement will be minimal to nil in this experience.

MODULE 2: Immersive Storytelling DLX - In this virtual inspection, the learner will be engaged in a compelling and relatable narrative right from the start. The immersive story will provide the motivation for the learner to do the inspection thoroughly and efficiently, by providing realistic real-world interactions and story elements. In addition to receiving the simple feedback provided in Module 1, the learner will also experience realistic outcomes that are directly related to their decisions (e.g. breakdown on the side the road after failing to identify a serious fluid leak).

Groups of learners will be invited to play one module or the other, and then the research lead will follow up with those learners immediately after they finish the DLX (and possibly again at specific time intervals after they finish, such as 1 week, 1 month, etc) to assess how much of the learning content they were able to retain. The data will then be compared to see if there is a difference, and assess how learners feel about their experience.

Assumptions / Known Constraints

  • Simulations developed under this plan are not intended to be played on tablets or cell phones.

  • These modules are not intended for teaching or learning of subject matter, but are instead intended to compare learning outcomes achieved using two different user engagement strategies.

  • These simulations will not be designed to play with VR headsets or controllers.

  • These will not be multiplayer simulations (i.e. students playing in pairs or the professor having the opportunity to be in the simulation with the student at the same time).
