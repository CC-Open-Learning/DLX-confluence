12 DEC 2024 - CE feedback from: Gino De Pinto, Karl Weiss

This week, the meeting focused on design discussions for the Biopsy Procedural segment. No feedback was received regarding demo build RT-24F8.0-11DEC2024. Instead, we discussed plans for student workflow and task structure for the procedural segment. Design decisions will be reflected in the official requirements and design pages, but I’ll touch on some of the key highlights here just for traceability:

Topic/Question

Discussion/Answer

Q: Should the Physician character wear red or dark green scrubs?

A (Gino): Conestoga RT students wear red scrubs, but faculty or hospital staff do not. Blue or green scrubs for the physician character would be good.

Q: When the physician requests 20cc of saline, what should the student look at?

Discussion (Karl and Gino):

  • First the scope is inserted into the patient airway (animation with narration). Student is looking at the bronch tower monitor to see the image from the scope.

  • Physician states, “We are in the right upper lobe.” Bronch Tower display monitor shows the correct image. Physician now requests saline.

  • Student looks at the procedure table to select the saline syringe, then looks at the bronchoscope to insert the syringe tip into the correct spot and see the plunger being pushed.

  • Then the student looks at the bronch tower display to see footage of airways being irrigated.

Q: Will we have procedural video on the screen in the DLX?

Discussion (all):

  • If we have usable media such as images or video, it is technically possible to show it on the Bronch Tower display monitor. Some discussion about where we would get the media, G & K do not think it would be suitable to show footage from the practice mannequin in the skills lab because it does not resemble real human anatomy. Everyone agrees that in a real procedure, the scope display is a big focal point, but it’s not clear if it is a primary objective for this DLX?

  • Karl says primary objectives for the DLX are

    • Student must work collaboratively with the physician throughout the procedure

    • Student should gain confidence and familiarity with the procedure steps and the equipment.  

  • This is not an anatomy lesson, but we can’t avoid showing realistic images on the display monitor or else it will look wrong.  Still images are probably adequate, updated as needed as we progress through the procedure steps.

  • Conclusion: The Bronch Tower display monitor cannot be BLANK or WRONG because that would distract the learner, but we do not need continuous video footage because it is not important to the learning objectives for this DLX. Still images will be updated as needed during the procedure.

Q: How much control should the student have over the camera during the DLX?  We can choose a default view, but do we offer the student the option of looking away?

A from Karl: There can be times when the student has camera control and they can look around, particularly when they are responding to a prompt or performing an action. But when we are showing an animation or other visual info that the student needs to see, the camera will move to show that in the scene and not allow the student to look away.

Examples discussed:

  • when we are showing the saline being administered or watching the airways on the bronch tower monitor, the student will not have camera controls - they can't look away when this info is being presented.

  • when the student is prompted to administer saline, we can give them control of the camera. They can look at the patient, the monitors, the procedure table, whatever they want.  It is up to them to respond to the prompt, they will look at whatever they need.

Storyboard Feedback re: Patient Vitals Check workflow

Karl: Whenever the RT checks the patient vitals, they need to make a decision based on what they see on the monitor. RT should evaluate the vitals and decide to Proceed with the procedure or Pause the procedure, because that is a real life decision.

If the patient vitals are normal (in range, ideal or not ideal but still okay) the correct answer is always to Proceed. If the RT chooses to stop the procedure in that case, they get immediate feedback from the Physician NPC.

If the patient vitals are poor (out of range) the correct answer is to Pause. If the RT chooses to Proceed in that case, they get immediate feedback from the Physician NPC.  While paused, the vitals should improve and then the procedure will continue from where we left off.

Ideally the vitals will change “randomly” throughout the procedure and the RT will need to respond to the situation. If the vitals are very bad and the RT student is not checking the vitals, we can have an audio/visual alarm prompt so that they know they need to look at the Vitals.

Vitals monitor will be present in the scene at all times, but the student needs to click on the monitor or on the Vitals button in the main toolbar to see the animation and “check” the vitals. The student cannot make decisions about proceed/pause without checking the vitals.

Technical Notes about Vitals Checks (not discussed in the meeting - discussed in project team chat after the meeting)

  • “Random” can really just mean that we have 4 different animations, each looping a specific state. NOTE that values shown here are suggestions only and must be confirmed with CEs before going into the requirements - this is just an example of ranges that could be shown for each state:

    • Stable:  All values are in range e.g. SpO2 at/above 96, HR between 62-76, BP between 118/72 - 126/88.  In this state, the correct answer is Proceed.
      NOTE: ALL PATIENTS must start the procedure with Vitals Stable, this is a requirement to begin the procedure.

    • Mild Distress:  SpO2 between 91-94, HR between 80-88, BP between 130/90 - 148/98. In this state, the student can choose to Proceed or Pause.

    • Critical: SpO2 between 85-90, elevated HR between 94-120, elevated BP between 156/104 - 166/116. In this state, the correct answer is Pause.

    • Recovery:  This animation will show a gradual (10-20 seconds) recovery period where patient vitals improve from Critical -> Stable or from Mild Distress -> Stable. This animation will only play when the Pause option is selected.

  • Student can click to check patient vitals anytime they want. Each time they click, we will randomly show Stable/Mild Distress/ Critical animation.

  • If the student doesn’t click to view vitals, a new animation can be selected every X minutes.

  • Every time the student clicks to view the patient vitals, they will have the opportunity to Report specific values on the screen - this will result in hearing a recording of the value being read out, e.g. "Heart rate is at 90". Reporting is optional, and can be done for any of the values, regardless if they are in range or not.  The physician NPC will respond to all reports - specific responses can be mapped later.  Clicking the Report option does not exit the Vitals view.

  • Every time the student clicks to view patient vitals, they must make a decision to Proceed or Pause the procedure.  This is the only way to exit the vitals view and return to the procedure. This decision can be made with or without Reporting any values.  Vitals will remain in the current pattern (looping) until the student makes a decision about whether to continue or pause the procedure (taking a very long time to decide does not count as "Pause" - they must actually select the Pause option)

    • Proceed: Continue with the procedure without pausing - this exits the Vitals view.  If the student selects this option when patient vitals are Mild Distress, no feedback is given, but this could result in patient deterioration (either an alarm prompt or patient vitals are degraded to Critical on next Vitals view). If the student selects this option when the patient vitals are Critical, they will get immediate feedback from the physician NPC.

    • Pause: Temporarily halt the procedure - this does not exit the Vitals view.  When this option is selected, the student will hear a recording of the recommendation to pause the procedure - this is separate from the Report of a specific value from the monitor.   The physician will respond to all Pause recommendations. 

      • If the student selects this option when patient vitals are stable, they will get immediate feedback from the physician NPC. e.g. “I think everything looks good, it doesn’t make sense to stop right now.”

      • If the student selects this option when patient vitals are Mild Distress or Critical, they will get confirmation from the physician NPC and the Recovery animation will start to play.  Note that the Patient NPC appearance does not change when the vitals state changes. Only the Vitals monitor animation is variable. At any point during the Recovery animation, the student can select Proceed (the Pause option is disabled while the procedure is Paused), but if the patient vitals have not recovered to an acceptable range, they will get immediate feedback from the Physician NPC.  Once the patient vitals have returned to acceptable range (tbd by CE’s), the student can select Proceed to exit the Vitals view.

Bronchoscope

  • We discussed the appearance of the bronchoscope in the Object Viewer. The concern is about the biopsy port and the suction valve, which are shown already installed on the bronchoscope. In the procedural segment for Setup, the students will need to identify and install these components in the bronchoscope.

  • Students need to be able to recognize that the Biopsy Port and Suction Valve are not part of the scope - they are separate components that need to be assembled in the scope.

  • Talked about how to best leverage the existing functionality of the Object Viewer, because the models are not interactable in the Object Viewer. Discussion about adding multiple different models (selectable in the Object Viewer or auto-toggle between different models) as a future upgrade to the Object Viewer feature. Currently, the viewer does not support loading multiple different models.  Discussed many different options - I won't describe them all here, but we arrived at a solution everyone likes:
    DECISION: 
    - When the student explores the Bronchoscope, we will show the Bronchoscope with all components installed (as it looks now) so that the students can see how it looks when it is fully assembled.  
    -  We will add individual models for the Biopsy Port and Suction Valve in the Bronchoscopy Supplies category in the Supply Cart Inventory.  When the student views these items in the inventory, each valve will be shown all by itself (just the valve, not the scope) so that the student can gain familiarity with how the valves look when they are not yet installed. We already have Item Name and Description for these items in the collaboration document, we will bring that information over to the development project and get these into the next demo.
    - Later, in the procedural segments of the DLX, the student will go through the whole process of setting up all of the equipment and supplies for a bronchoscopy procedure, and they will need to identify the suction valve and biopsy port AND decide where to install them on the bronchoscope in that segment. This will not be covered in Orientation.
